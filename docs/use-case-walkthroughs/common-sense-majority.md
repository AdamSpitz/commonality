# Discovering the common-sense majority

Most people aren't hardcore ideologues. Most people think of themselves as reasonable, common-sense kinds of people — and they're mostly right. But they can't see each other. The loudest voices dominate, the media highlights the extremes, and the ordinary person who holds nuanced views has no way to find out that millions of other people feel the same way.

This walkthrough is about what happens when they can.


## Seeding

Various people, independently, sign statements in their own language:

- From the right: **"I'm tired of being told I'm evil for disagreeing."**
- From the left: **"I want to persuade, not just preach to the choir."**
- From somewhere in the middle: **"Most people are reasonable; the loudest voices aren't representative."**
- Practical: **"I'd pay for political content that doesn't make me angry."**

Each of these is genuine. Each reflects something the signer actually feels. None of them require the signer to identify as "moderate" or join any kind of coalition.


## Connection

The implication graph — the system's way of connecting related statements — notices that these all point toward something like **"constructive cross-partisan discourse is worth supporting."**

Nobody had to agree on that wording. Nobody had to join a group. Nobody even had to know about the other statements. The system just discovered the overlap.


## Visibility

The statement page now shows 50,000 direct signers — and 2 million indirect supporters. Those are people who signed their own statements, in their own words, and the implication graph connected them.

That number is itself the news.

Think about what it means to a person who's been feeling isolated — who thinks they're the only one in their social circle who's tired of the anger, who wants to engage honestly with people they disagree with. They visit the page and see: **two million people feel the same way.**

Not two million people who joined a movement. Not two million people who were asked to sign a petition. Two million people who, independently, expressed something they cared about — and the system revealed that they were all saying versions of the same thing.

The common-sense majority has always been there. It just couldn't see itself.


## Action

Now that the demand is visible, things start to happen:

- **A funding portal** for this cause now has real demand signal. There's money behind it — pledges from people who said they'd support this kind of thing.
- **Delegates** start directing funds toward specific projects and content that serve this cause.
- **Content creators** see the portal and start targeting it — writing the kind of thoughtful, noninflammatory content that people said they wanted.
- **Projects emerge** — maybe a media outlet that commits to the standard, maybe a set of AI evaluation tools, maybe a directory of writers who consistently produce good work.

The discovery that millions of people agree on something creates the conditions for actually doing something about it.


## What the statements actually look like

On any given polarized issue, we expect to see a recurring structure:

- **Pole statements** — the positions held by the loudest voices on each side, the ones that define the "two sides" in public discourse
- **Normal-people-from-each-side statements** — what most people on each side actually think, which is usually more nuanced than the pole
- **A commonality statement** — the position that both normal-people statements imply, which is probably the supermajority position if anyone bothered to ask

The point isn't that "moderate" or "compromise" positions are always right. The point is that when a supermajority position *exists* but is *invisible* — because the political system bundles it with other things and teaches each side that the other side is full of nutty extremists — that's a bug the system fixes. Let's at least *notice* the common ground.

### The sub-patterns

The gap between pole statements and the hidden commonality takes different shapes depending on what's causing it:

**Compromise in the middle.** Genuine preference differences, but with an overlap zone. The moderate-from-each-side statements are "I'd prefer..." while the commonality statement is "I'd be okay with it if..." — not either side's ideal, but fine with the moderates on both sides.

Example: On abortion, the moderate left might prefer availability until at least 12 weeks; the moderate right might prefer at most 16 weeks. So the commonality is somewhere in the 12–16 week range — not exactly what either side wants, but acceptable to both.

**No major controversy among normal people.** A vocal minority (sometimes one on each side) creates the illusion of a controversy that barely exists. The commonality statement isn't a compromise — it's just the obvious consensus that almost everyone already holds but nobody realizes is shared.

Example: Most people on both left and right think the police should be accountable when they abuse power *and* that we obviously shouldn't defund the police. The extremists ("defund!" vs. "back the blue no matter what") get disproportionate coverage, making it seem like "the other side" disagrees with what is actually a near-universal position.

**Same values, different beliefs.** People who would agree with the other side if the facts were different. The commonality statement is conditional: "If X is true, then Y." This doesn't resolve the policy conflict, but it transforms it from "you're evil" to "you're misinformed" — which is less heated and more productive, because you can actually try to persuade someone about facts.

Example: "If rehabilitating criminals works well, great, we should do more of it. If it doesn't work, don't keep releasing repeat offenders." Left and right might disagree on the facts, but the underlying values are shared.

**Misunderstandings of what the other side believes.** One side's actual position is something the other side would largely agree with, but the other side doesn't know that's the position. The commonality statement is corrective — it states what the side actually believes, in a way the other side can recognize as reasonable.

This is the hardest sub-pattern, because the people who hold the misunderstanding won't spontaneously sign a statement they haven't seriously considered. This is where [noninflammatory content](./noninflammatory-content.md) comes in — it provides both the incentive and the mechanism for communicating across the divide, getting sympathetically-framed statements in front of people who haven't encountered them.

**Different problems, same solution.** The two sides arrive at the same policy from completely different motivations. The commonality statement is the policy itself, stated without either side's justification. This pattern is especially actionable: the policy gets signed by people from both sides, making it fundable, even though the supporters' *reasoning* is completely different.

Example: Breaking up big tech — the left says monopoly power hurts consumers; the right says platforms censor conservative voices. Both arrive at "break them up."

### Conditional support

Across several of these sub-patterns, people may be unwilling to sign a commonality statement unless they're confident the other side is signing in good faith. A left-leaning person might think: "If I admit I don't want to defund the police, the right will use my admission as an excuse to ignore actual abuses of police power." And vice versa.

So commonality statements may need to be structured as mutual assurances — "I care about X *and* Y, and as long as both left and right are bipartisanly working towards both, I'm against the extremists on my own side." Whether this needs an explicit mechanism (something like an assurance contract for mutual commitments) or whether just writing it into the language of the statements is sufficient is an open question.


## What this illustrates

**Statements are the entry point.** You don't pledge money, you don't join anything — you just say what you believe. That's the lowest-friction action possible, and it's the foundation that everything else builds on.

**The implication graph discovers coalitions that nobody built.** The left-coded statement and the right-coded statement weren't designed to connect. An AI noticed that they point toward a common cause. That's the organic coalition: not organized, not negotiated — discovered.

**Supporter counting reveals latent demand.** The 2 million number isn't a vanity metric. It's demand signal. It tells content creators "there's an audience for this" and tells delegates "there's money behind this."

**The emotional core is recognition.** The most powerful moment isn't when money flows or projects launch. It's when a person sees that number and realizes: *oh — there are millions of us. We just couldn't see each other before.*
