# A neighborhood throws a block party

A few families on a street want to throw a proper summer block party — live music, food, bounce castle, the works. It would cost $1,500. Worth it if the whole street chips in; not worth it if only five families do.

Here's how it plays out.


## The assurance contract

One of the families sets up a pledge: "Summer block party — $1,500 goal. If we hit the goal by June 1st, the money goes to the party. If not, everyone gets refunded."

That "if not, everyone gets refunded" is the key. Nobody has to be the sucker who puts money in before knowing if enough others will join. You pledge, and if it doesn't happen, you lose nothing.

Within a week, forty households have pledged an average of $40 each. It funds.


## Delegation makes it easier

But not every household wants to make individual decisions about every neighborhood event. Seven of the more active neighbors have become informal delegates — people others trust to make these kinds of calls. Between them, they have small delegations from most of the other households on the street: "Here's $20/month for neighborhood stuff; use your judgment."

When the block party pledge goes up, those seven delegates can commit their delegated funds immediately. That gets the pledge to critical mass fast, which encourages the remaining households to pile on. The forty households didn't all need to independently decide to participate; seven people who pay attention to neighborhood life made it happen.


## After the party

The party is a hit. Now there's an opportunity for retroactive funding: households that didn't pledge beforehand — maybe they were out of town, maybe they were skeptical — see the photos, hear the stories, and want to show support. They can fund the project after the fact. Part of what they contribute flows back to the delegate who organized it and the families who pledged early, as a small reward for taking the initiative.

That reward is tiny in absolute terms, but it matters for a different reason: it establishes a pattern. The next time someone proposes a neighborhood project — a community garden, a street cleanup, a holiday lights display — there's already a network of delegates, a precedent of successful funding, and a group of people who know the drill.


## What this illustrates

**Assurance contracts solve real coordination problems at any scale.** The block party was never going to fail because people didn't want it. It was going to fail because nobody wanted to be the one chasing down payments, and nobody wanted to pay if not enough others would. The pledge-and-refund structure eliminates both problems.

**Delegation means a few engaged people can carry the neighborhood.** Not everyone needs to pay attention. The seven active neighbors act as a layer of initiative that makes things happen without requiring everyone to be involved in every decision.

**Retroactive funding rewards initiative.** The families who pledged early — who took the (small, refundable) risk — get recognized when latecomers contribute. That creates a culture where taking initiative pays off, which means more initiative next time.

**Small things add up.** Any one block party is trivial. But a neighborhood where funding small public goods is routine and frictionless is a meaningfully better place to live. The argument isn't that any single block party changes the world — it's that there are a *lot* of small public goods like this that simply aren't getting produced because the coordination cost is too high. Make the coordination cost near-zero, and you get a lot more nice things.
