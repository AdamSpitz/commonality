# Elevator pitch

Escalating pitches for [Common Sense Majority](../README.md): 10 seconds buys you 30 seconds, which buys you 2 minutes, which buys you 5. The boring part is "moderates exist" — everyone knows that. The interesting part is why this time is different.


## What's actually novel (reference material for pitching)

### The core differentiator: credible neutrality as infrastructure

  - **Every moderate movement dies because of the trust problem.** Both sides need to believe the system isn't captured by the other side. That's not a nice-to-have — it's structurally necessary for anything cross-partisan. And no organization can credibly promise it, because organizations can be captured.
  - **A protocol can't be captured.** There's no "Commonality Inc." to sue, no bank accounts to freeze, no editor to bribe, no board to infiltrate. Money flows onchain via smart contracts. There's literally nobody to capture.
  - **AI with open-source prompts is a new kind of referee.** Content evaluation, bridge-finding, implication detection — all done by AI whose prompts you can read. If you don't trust the operator, run your own. The subjectivity is transparent, not hidden behind editorial discretion.
  - **You choose who you trust at every level.** Attesters, delegates, nudgers — all configurable. The system discovers common ground *despite* people having different trusted sources, which is what makes the common ground genuine.

### The mechanisms that didn't exist before

  - **Crowdfunding an *adjective*, not a person.** You can fund "noninflammatory content" as a category. Legacy systems can't do this — you'd have to pick a specific creator and hope they don't sell out. Here, you fund content *that meets a standard*, as evaluated by attesters you choose.
  - **AI that actively synthesizes common ground.** Not just moderating — reading what moderate people on opposite sides wrote and noticing they don't actually conflict. Then proposing bridge statements where each side signs their own version (still clearly their side), and the common ground is *implied* without anyone betraying their tribe.
  - **Indirect supporter counts.** Nobody signs the same petition. People write in their own words. The implication graph (made possible by AI that's patient enough to handle a high volume of legwork) connects them and produces a number no existing system can: "50,000 direct signers, 2 million indirect supporters." That number is itself the news.

### Game theory that forces adoption

  - **Both sides need insurance against being out of power.** Government funding is unstable — whatever you fund today gets defunded when the other side wins. Putting critical funding on uncapturable infrastructure is a dominant strategy *regardless* of what the other side does.
  - **Assurance contracts as a zero-cost strike fund.** Visibly demonstrate your community's funding capacity without spending a dollar. The pledges sit there, shifting negotiations. If the threat is never called, everyone gets refunded.
  - **Competitive pressure.** If the other side adopts better infrastructure and you don't, they're raising more money and building capacity you can't touch. Sequential cascade, not grand bargain.

### Growth dynamics

  - **Walking up a hill, not scaling a cliff.** Every step produces immediate value — unlike a party, where years of work produce zero until you win an election. Sign a statement → it's counted. Pledge money → it goes to exactly what you chose (or refunds). No lottery tickets.
  - **The coalition nobody had to build.** A progressive signs one thing, a conservative signs another, the system discovers they agree. No meeting, no negotiation, no compromise on wording.

## Anticipated questions

**"So you're some sort of fund?"** No — we built infrastructure through which anyone can contribute to funding noninflammatory content. There's no organization holding the money; it flows through smart contracts.

**"So you're some sort of media company?"** No — this funds normal social-media content on X, YouTube, Substack, wherever. Any writer can earn from the pool. We're not creating content; we're creating a system that lets the crowd fund content that meets a standard.

**"What makes it noninflammatory?"** Ultimately you decide — or a delegate you've chosen to handle your funding decisions. AI does the legwork (open-source prompts, published reasoning), giving you a feed of candidates. You're mostly just reading what popped up and clicking "yup, I'll put a buck towards that one."

**"Why would I trust the AI?"** You don't have to. The prompts are open-source — read them. The reasoning is published alongside each decision. If you still don't trust it, configure a different evaluator or run your own. The system works precisely because nobody has to agree on a single referee.


## 2-minute follow-up

"Okay, so how does it actually work?"

**The entry point is dead simple.** You write a statement — in your own words — saying what you actually believe. "I think housing should be built faster, even if it means relaxing zoning." "I'd support school choice if the public schools were protected too." "Immigration enforcement is fine, but the legal process needs to be way faster." Whatever you actually think. You don't join anything, you don't sign someone else's petition, you don't adopt someone else's framing.

**Then the AI finds the overlaps.** The system has an implication graph — AI reads the universe of statements and publishes connections: "this statement implies that one." So a progressive who wrote about wanting to persuade rather than preach, and a conservative who wrote about wanting a fair hearing, get connected — automatically, without either of them knowing about the other or agreeing on wording. The statement page shows: "50,000 direct signers, 2 million indirect supporters." Those 2 million didn't sign this exact statement — they signed their own statements, in their own words, that the system recognized as pointing toward the same thing. That indirect count is a number no existing system can produce. Polls only ask the questions pollsters choose. Petitions only count people who agreed to one exact wording. This is bottom-up demand aggregated across millions of people's idiosyncratic language.

**On the hardest issues, the AI goes further.** Take abortion. A moderate leftie writes: "I want abortion to be available so women aren't forced into a pregnancy they don't want." A moderate rightie writes: "Late-term abortion is horrific." The AI notices these don't actually conflict — and synthesizes a bridge. Each side gets a "nudged" version of their own statement that's still clearly *their* side's statement, but that implies a common-ground position (say, "allowed in the first trimester, forbidden after that, let's settle this"). The critical move: nobody signs the common-ground statement directly. You sign *your side's* version. The common ground is implied by both, but nobody feels like they're betraying their tribe. Face is saved. And this works for the hard cases specifically because the statements are structured as bilateral assurances: "I care about X, but I also agree with Y *as long as you also agree with X*." The commitment is conditional, so neither side feels like they're conceding unilaterally.

**The delivery mechanism is crowdfunded noninflammatory content.** How do people even encounter these bridge statements? Through social-media content — on X, YouTube, Substack, wherever — that's been funded from a pool of money specifically dedicated to content that communicates across the political divide without being inflammatory. You can contribute $10/month and never think about it again — delegate your funding decisions to someone you trust. The content is evaluated by AI: "Would a reasonable person from the other side be able to engage with this without feeling attacked?" The prompts are open-source, the reasoning is posted alongside the decision, and you choose which evaluator you trust. Note what's new here: you're funding an *adjective* — "noninflammatory" — not a specific person or publication. Legacy systems can't do that. You'd have to find a content creator and hope they don't sell out. Here, you fund content *that meets a standard*, and any writer can earn from the pool.

**Every step produces value.** Unlike a political party, where years of organizing accomplish nothing until you win an election, every action here takes effect immediately. Sign a statement — it's counted. Pledge money — it goes to exactly what you chose, or refunds. The growth curve is walking up a hill, not scaling a cliff.


## 5-minute follow-up

"Why would anyone actually adopt this?"

**Both sides need insurance against being out of power.** Government funding is inherently unstable — whatever your side funds today gets defunded when the other side wins next. Putting your community's critical funding on infrastructure that nobody can shut down is a dominant strategy regardless of your politics.

**It's costless to start.** Assurance contracts let you visibly demonstrate funding capacity without spending a dollar. "500 families have pledged $2M that activates if the province cuts our grant." If the threat is never called, everyone gets refunded. It's a zero-cost strike fund. The pledges sit there, shifting negotiations.

**Adoption cascades — it doesn't require a grand bargain.** One side adopts first out of necessity (their funding got threatened, their community needs independence from a hostile government). They discover the mechanisms are also just *better* — raise more money, fund more effectively, fully transparent. The other side sees them pulling ahead and adopts too. Sequential cascade, not grand bargain. Competitive pressure does the work.

**The coalition builds itself.** A progressive signs one thing, a conservative signs another, the system discovers they agree. No meeting, no negotiation, no compromise on wording. The cross-partisan constituency emerges bottom-up — with countable supporters and visible funding capacity — and that's a political fact that changes calculations. "Ten million people across the political spectrum have supported this position, and there's $50M in pledges behind aligned projects" isn't a petition. It's a demonstrated economic force.

**It routes around the political system rather than confronting it.** Rather than fighting for control, you make parts of the existing system increasingly irrelevant. Every function a community funds independently is one less lever that a hostile government can pull. You don't need to win an election, form a party, or persuade political opponents. You need people signing statements, pledging small amounts of money, and delegating to people they trust.
