# Why trust it?

It's reasonable to ask: why would I put money into a system I don't control? Here's the answer.

## The code is open-source

Anyone can read the smart contracts that govern how pledges are held, released, and refunded. There's no hidden logic. If you're technically inclined, you can verify exactly what the code does before you interact with it. If you're not, you can rely on others who are — because open-source code gets scrutinized publicly, not just by the organization that wrote it.

## All transactions are public

Every pledge, every refund, every funding decision, every attestation — all of it is recorded on a public blockchain. You can verify any claim the system makes. If a project says it received $50,000, you can check. If a delegate says they directed funds toward education, you can see exactly where each dollar went. Nobody can falsify the record after the fact.

## Nobody controls it

There's no company with admin keys, no organization that could shut it down or redirect funds, no single point of failure. The smart contracts run on a public blockchain; once deployed, they execute their logic regardless of what any organization decides. The team that built Commonality can't change how your pledge works once it's made.

## Assurance contracts mean you risk nothing

When you pledge to a project, your money goes into an assurance contract — a piece of code that holds the funds and releases them only if the project hits its funding threshold by its deadline. If the threshold isn't met, the contract refunds you automatically. Nobody makes that decision; the code makes it. You can't get defrauded by a project that "just missed" its goal — the refund is guaranteed by the contract, not by anyone's honesty.

## Delegation is revocable

If you delegate to someone and you disagree with their choices, you can take your money back. Not "request a refund" — actually take it back, unilaterally, at any time. The funds you've pledged but your delegate hasn't yet directed toward a project are still yours to reclaim.

## Bridges to traditional finance *(coming, not yet built)*

The system is designed to coexist with traditional financial and legal structures, not replace them. This infrastructure is on the roadmap but not yet implemented — for now, participation requires holding crypto directly. When bridges are built, practically this will mean:

- **Credit card donations work.** Bridge operators (charities, project organizers, or dedicated services) accept traditional payments and record them onchain on the donor's behalf. Donors who don't want to touch crypto don't have to.
- **Tax receipts still work.** A registered charity can act as intermediary: the donor makes a tax-deductible donation to the charity, and the charity buys tokens on the donor's behalf. The donor gets a tax receipt from the charity and onchain recognition from the project.
- **Government matching funds can participate.** A government agency can act as a bridge operator — when a project reaches a threshold, the agency contributes matching funds onchain, visible to everyone alongside the individual pledges.
- **Traditional and onchain funding coexist.** A single project can accept donations from all of the above simultaneously. The assurance contract doesn't care whether the ETH came from a direct onchain pledge or through a bridge operator. The threshold, refund logic, and accountability are unified regardless.

The design intent: you won't have to choose between using Commonality and the parts of the financial world you're already comfortable with.

## It doesn't take a side

Commonality isn't built for one political tribe. Both sides of most political divides have independent reasons to use it.

When you're out of power, your causes are vulnerable — grants get pulled, funding redirected, charities audited. Building funding capacity outside government control is insurance. And since assurance contracts refund automatically if a threshold isn't met, setting up that backup costs you nothing unless it's actually needed.

When you're in power, the mechanisms are just better infrastructure: assurance contracts raise more than unconditional donation appeals, delegation gets more people participating, transparency builds donor trust. Your side funds more effective projects regardless of who's winning elections.

The result: people on opposite sides of the political spectrum often end up funding the same project — because they care about the same outcome, even when they frame it differently. The system doesn't ask you to agree on *why* something matters. It just finds where you agree on *what should happen*.
