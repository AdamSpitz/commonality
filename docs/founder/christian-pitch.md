# Pitch for Christians

I want a pitch for Christians. Not because this project is aimed specifically at Christians, but just because I have a lot of Christian friends, and I think might find value in this project, but many of them are skeptical of the crazy crypto/AI stuff.

They're not exactly averse to tech, they're Internet-savvy enough, but they also live in the real world rather than the online world. They go to church, they donate to the church and to the various causes that the church collects for, but I think they'd be skeptical at first of any pitch along the lines of "come use this weird computerized protocol instead."

Various points:
  - Christians already do lots of charitable giving, often through the church; this is meant as rails for that. (Keep doing what you're doing! LazyGiving does the bookkeeping. Alignment is a dashboard for the church's projects.)
  - But there's a lot of potential here for *larger-scale* coordination on topics the church cares a lot about. You don't need fancy coordination tools for small projects within the church - the pastor just gets up and tells everyone "here's what we're collecting for." But coordination of *multi-church* projects and wider-world church-aligned projects is *much* harder. That's what these tools are really meant for. Coordination between many churches, without needing to organize into a formal hierarchy (with all the problems that comes with). And coordination *at scale* to do bigger stuff - the wider culture is very secular and very leftist, and you might feel frustrated that the wider Christian community in general has been doing a less-than-spectacularly-effective job of pushing back against that. Christianity is great at making sure that the orphans in the local community get fed, but not so great at coordinating at scale within the wider society. You need better coordination tools.

# A pitch for Christians

## Start with what you already do

You already give. You put something in the plate, you write the check for the building fund, you cover a family's groceries when the pastor mentions they're struggling, you sponsor the mission trip. None of that needs fixing, and this isn't a pitch to replace it. It's a pitch for better rails underneath it.

Think of it in three plain pieces:

  - **LazyGiving does the bookkeeping.** Keep giving exactly the way you give now. The system just keeps an honest, automatic record — who gave, where it went, whether the project actually came together — so nobody has to chase receipts or take it on faith that the money landed where it was meant to. Cheerful giving and clean books aren't in tension; this just removes the friction that makes the books a chore.
  - **Alignment is a dashboard for the church's projects.** Instead of finding out about the food drive or the crisis-pregnancy center or the well-digging fund only when it happens to come up from the front, you can see the projects your church (and churches like it) are working on, in one place, and put your weight behind the ones that move you.
  - **Assurance contracts handle the "only if enough of us show up" problem.** This is the one genuinely new tool, and it's not exotic. You've felt the situation it solves a hundred times.

## The problem assurance contracts solve

Say the youth program needs $1,500 to keep running next year. You'd happily give $50 — but only if it's actually going to come together. If the church only scrapes up $400, your $50 didn't save the program; it just went into a pot that fell short, and now you're out $50 with nothing to show for it. So everybody hangs back, waiting to see if it's "really going to happen," and good things die for lack of a first mover.

An assurance contract is just a promise with a safety net: *"I'll give $50 if we reach $1,500 by the deadline — and if we don't, I get my money back, no harm done."* Everyone can commit boldly because nobody risks throwing good money after a project that won't reach escape velocity. It turns out this is an old, well-understood idea (it's how Kickstarter works), and it's quietly one of the most useful tools ever invented for getting a group of people to fund something together when each of them is reasonably waiting on the others.

This is the kind of coordination the church has always been good at — at small scale. When it's one congregation and one need, you don't need any of this; the pastor stands up and says "here's what we're collecting for," and that *is* the coordination mechanism. The tool only earns its keep when the group gets too big for one person at a podium to hold together.

## Where this actually matters: coordinating at scale

Here's the honest assessment. The church is *excellent* at the orphan down the street, the widow in the third pew, the family whose house burned down. That kind of love-your-neighbor giving is the church operating at its best, and it has never needed software.

But ask a harder question: how good is the broader Christian world at coordinating *across* congregations, *across* towns, on the things it claims to care about most? Much of the surrounding culture is secular and pulls hard in one direction, and it would be fair to feel that the Christian response — for all its numbers and conviction — has been fragmented and outmatched. Not for lack of people or money or sincerity. For lack of a way to point all of it the same direction at the same time without first building a giant bureaucracy.

That's the gap. Coordinating ten thousand people across two hundred churches is a fundamentally different problem than passing the plate, and the church's traditional tools don't reach it. The usual fix is to build an institution — a denomination, a national org, a 501(c)(3) with a staff and a board and an overhead rate — and everyone knows the failure modes there: it gets political, it gets captured, it spends half its donations on itself, it answers to its biggest funders rather than its mission, and it becomes one more thing to be skeptical of.

Commonality is a way to get the *coordination* without building the *institution*. Many churches can pull together on a shared project without organizing into a hierarchy, without anyone being in charge of everyone else, without a single office that can be pressured or co-opted. Each congregation, each family, each person commits on their own — and the commitments add up into something at a scale the church usually can't reach without surrendering to a bureaucracy first.

## "Isn't this just the crypto stuff I'm skeptical of?"

Reasonable instinct, and you're right to hold it. Most of what flies under the "crypto/AI" banner deserves the skepticism. So let's be plain about what this is and isn't.

It is *not* an investment, a coin to buy, a thing that goes up and down, or a way to get rich. There's nothing to speculate on. It's plumbing for giving away money you've already earned and already paid tax on — after-tax dollars you already control, going where you already wanted them to go.

The screens are meant to look like the giving apps and banking apps you already use, not like a crypto trading terminal. (Candidly: making the experience genuinely that simple is still work in progress — but that's the explicit target, not an afterthought. If it's scary, it's not done.) You will not need to understand the machinery underneath, the same way you don't need to understand how the offering envelope gets counted, or how your bank moves a wire, to trust that it works.

And it coexists with everything you do now. Your church keeps its charity, its tax-deductible receipts, its existing accounts. Someone who never wants to touch any of this can give by ordinary card or check and a bridge handles the rest. Nobody has to go "full crypto," now or ever.

## Why the shape of this fits the church

One last thing, less of a sales point than an observation you may find you already agree with.

The New Testament church is strikingly *bottom-up*. Gifts distributed according to need, decisions made in local bodies, authority that serves rather than lords over, a network of congregations bound by shared conviction rather than a single chain of command. It is not an org chart. It's something more like a web of trust — people who know and vouch for one another, specialization by gift, mutual aid that doesn't wait for a central office to authorize it.

That's almost exactly the shape of what this technology is good at. Decentralized systems are, at bottom, a way for a large number of people to coordinate and keep honest records *without* a central authority everyone has to trust and obey. The reason that matters more now than it would have two thousand years ago is simple arithmetic: "the local body" used to be a village you could see across. Today a city holds millions and the wider world holds billions, and coordinating bottom-up at *that* scale — without defaulting to either a government or a bureaucracy to do it for you — genuinely required tools that didn't exist until recently.

You don't have to find that theologically interesting to use the thing. But if you've ever felt that the church's witness gets diluted the moment it tries to organize at scale — that something good gets lost when love-your-neighbor becomes a department with a budget — this is an attempt to let the giving scale up *without* losing the bottom-up character that made it the church's giving in the first place.

So: keep doing what you're doing. This just gives it better rails, an honest ledger, and — when you want it — a way to stand together with far more people than one congregation could ever fit in a room.
