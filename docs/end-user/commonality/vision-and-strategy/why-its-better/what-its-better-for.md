# What kinds of public goods is Commonality better for?

We already have governments and charities funding public goods. The world is not devoid of clean water, research, or parks. So the honest question isn't "could a public good exist without Commonality?" — obviously many do. It's: **which public goods are *underproduced* today, why, and which of those does Commonality actually fix?**

A topic list ("environment! research! infrastructure!") is the wrong answer, because the same topic can be well-served or badly-served depending on *why* it's getting funded or not. The right answer is about which **failure mode** is binding.

## The principle

Every underproduced public good is underproduced for a *reason*, and our existing institutions fail in characteristic ways. Commonality helps exactly where its mechanisms attack the specific failure that's biting — and not otherwise. The one sentence to remember:

> **Taxation funds what a central authority can be made to *approve*. Commonality funds what people are individually *willing to pay for*.**

So Commonality outproduces the status quo for goods that have **willing payers who are currently blocked** — blocked by majoritarianism, polarization, gatekeepers, jurisdictional mismatch, prospective-funding risk, censorship, or distrust.

The binding constraint shifts: from *"can you get permission / win the vote?"* to *"can you find enough people who care to pay?"* Commonality wins for every good where the second question is easier to answer than the first — and once you start asking it, the category turns out to be far larger than people assume. Even goods we reflexively file under "obviously needs taxes" often have plenty of willing payers; a town that wants sewage will clear an [assurance-contract](../credible-solution/assurance-contracts.md) threshold without much trouble. The real test isn't the topic, it's whether the people who benefit are willing and able to pay — and for a remarkable range of public goods, they are.

## The kinds it's better for

Organized not by topic but by *what's blocking them today*:

**1. Minority-passion goods.** A dedicated minority cares intensely, but can't command a tax-funded majority and never will — niche cultural or scholarly preservation, enthusiast infrastructure, specific community or religious goods, unconventional research. Government structurally *can't* serve these (no majority to approve the spending); charity can, but eats the [free-rider](../credible-solution/assurance-contracts.md) and [discovery](../credible-solution/discovery.md) problems. This is the heartland — and it's not fringe. Most of what people genuinely care about and government ignores lives here.

**2. Polarization-trapped goods (hidden majorities).** Broadly wanted, but trapped inside a culture-war bundle so the political system can't deliver them. Commonality's [organic-coalition discovery](./organic-coalitions.md) is purpose-built for this, and essentially nothing else does it: it surfaces the concrete outcome people agree on without forcing them to share a framing.

**3. Sub-jurisdictional / hyper-local goods.** Too small or too specific for government to bother with — this block's park, this town's trail. Denser trust networks and lower pledge thresholds make these [especially natural for Commonality](../so-what/local-government.md).

**4. Orphaned cross-boundary goods.** The mirror image of #3: the beneficiaries don't map onto any *one* jurisdiction, so no government internalizes the full benefit. Inter-town infrastructure, open-source software, internet commons, global health. No single authority has the incentive to fund the whole thing; a permissionless protocol that pools willing payers across boundaries does.

**5. Gatekept / unconventional goods.** Good ideas that don't fit a grant category, come from people without credentials or connections, or are too novel or interdisciplinary to survive a review panel. [Permissionless creation plus crowdsourced filtering](./openness.md) means a project that the committee would have rejected can still find its funders.

**6. Proven-but-unpredictable goods.** Domains where you genuinely *can't* pick winners in advance but *can* recognize them afterward, and where attempts are cheap while hits are rare: creative content, open source, journalism, much research. [Retroactive funding](./retroactive-funding.md) is the mechanism that converts "I won't risk funding this" into "I'll reward what demonstrably worked."

**7. Politically-disfavored / suppressible goods.** Advocacy, legal defense, contested speech — things a hostile authority actively wants to *defund*. [Censorship-resistance](../hard-to-stop/censorship-resistance.md) turns "can't" into "can."

**8. Bridging / communication goods.** A genuinely new category: funding the spread of depolarizing, noninflammatory content across a divide. Government and charity essentially don't — and couldn't credibly — do this.

Notice these are eight angles on the *same* move. Minority passion, hidden majorities, localism, retroactive funding, censorship-resistance — each is "a willing payer who was blocked, now unblocked." That's why the principle, not the list, is the real content.

## Why this is the more useful framing

It's tempting to answer "what is Commonality better for?" with a list of topics. But the topic is rarely what determines whether a good gets produced — the *blockage* is. Two towns can both "need" the same thing; the one where willing payers can find each other and clear a threshold gets it, and the one where the funding has to survive a committee or a majority vote may not. Reframing around blockage rather than topic is what reveals how large the territory actually is: a great deal of what we underproduce is underproduced not because nobody would pay, but because the path from willingness to funding is broken. Commonality's job is to repair that path.
