# What kinds of public goods is Commonality better for?

We already have governments and charities funding public goods. The world is not devoid of clean water, research, or parks. So the honest question isn't "could a public good exist without Commonality?" — obviously many do. But our existing institutions fail in characteristic ways, and Commonality has mechanisms with different strengths and weaknesses. Which kinds of public goods are *underproduced* today, why, and which of those might Commonality be good for?

## The kinds it's better for

Organized not by topic but by *what's blocking them today* — each a specific failure mode of legacy institutions, paired with the specific Commonality mechanism that addresses it. These categories overlap rather than partition: a given good can sit in several at once.

**1. Self-financed minority goods.** A dedicated minority cares intensely and is willing to pay for the thing itself — niche cultural or scholarly preservation, enthusiast infrastructure, specific community or religious goods, unconventional research.

It's tempting to say "government structurally can't serve these — no majority to approve the spending." That's wrong, and instructively so: government *over*-serves plenty of intense minorities. Farm subsidies, occupational licensing, the sugar lobby — these are minority passions that command tax money against majority indifference, exactly because concentrated benefits plus diffuse costs make lobbying worth organizing and opposition not worth bothering with.

The real distinction is between *rent-seeking* and *self-financing*. The minorities the political system serves are extracting **private rents** — a subsidy or protection the group itself captures, excludable and concentrated, which is what makes the lobbying pay off. The minorities it won't serve are the ones who want a genuinely **public** good for their niche: non-excludable, benefiting everyone in the niche whether they chip in or not. There the minority faces a free-rider problem *within itself*, so it can't readily organize even the lobbying — let alone the funding.

So the honest framing isn't "passion can't outvote a majority." It's: the political system rewards minorities who can extract from the majority, and there is no good mechanism for a minority to fund its own genuinely public good out of its own pockets. The lobbying crowd wants *other people's* money; these minorities are willing to spend *their own* but are blocked by the [free-rider](../credible-solution/assurance-contracts.md) and [discovery](../credible-solution/discovery.md) problems — exactly the two problems Commonality's assurance contracts and discovery are built to solve. There's a legitimacy dividend, too: the rent-seeking version is coercive and often zero-sum (the majority pays for a minority's pet thing), while this is voluntary and self-financed. That's *why* it's underproduced — the voluntary version eats a free-rider problem the coercive version sidesteps with state power.

**2. Polarization-trapped goods (hidden majorities).** Broadly wanted, but trapped inside a culture-war bundle so the political system can't deliver them. Two mechanisms matter here, and the second is the stronger one. First, [organic-coalition discovery](./organic-coalitions.md) surfaces the concrete outcome people agree on without forcing them to share a framing. But discovery isn't enough on its own: even once a hidden majority can *see* itself, every large funder that might champion the good is itself polarized — a government is left or right, a big charity is coded one way or the other. What's missing in legacy-land is **institution-less aggregation**: a way to pool moderately-left and moderately-right money without a single unifying organization that both sides distrust. Assurance contracts are exactly that — they can take some money from left-leaning donors and some from right-leaning donors, and no one org has to own the whole thing. Retroactive funding works the same way: some receipts get bought up by people on the left, some by people on the right, with no need for a single big backer to take it all.

**3. Scale-mismatched goods.** Goods that don't line up with any one jurisdiction — two faces of the same problem. The first: things *too small or specific* for government to bother with — this block's park, this town's trail. The second, its mirror image: things whose beneficiaries don't map onto any *one* jurisdiction at all — inter-town infrastructure, open-source software, internet commons, global health — so no single authority internalizes the full benefit and none has the incentive to fund the whole thing.

The honest version of this claim is narrower than it first looks. Commonality doesn't grant you the legal right to take up a collection and pave the road; government legally monopolizes many of these goods, and a protocol doesn't change that. Where the good is *permitted but neglected*, Commonality just does it better. Where it's legally monopolized, this is a credible-threat or long-term structural-change play, not an immediate fix. But the underlying advantage is real and genuinely differentiating: a fixed federal → state → municipal hierarchy can only coordinate at its predefined tiers, whereas [Commonality coordinates at arbitrary scales and across boundaries](../so-what/local-government.md) — pooling willing payers wherever they happen to be.

**4. Gatekept / unconventional goods.** Good ideas that don't fit a grant category, come from people without credentials or connections, or are too novel or interdisciplinary to survive a review panel. [Permissionless creation plus crowdsourced filtering](./openness.md) means a project the committee would have rejected can still find its funders.

The clean analogy: **Commonality is to public goods what startups are to private goods.** In the world of private goods, when the big incumbents are too stodgy to see a good idea, you don't have to convince them — you start a company and appeal directly to the market by *demonstrating* value rather than getting permission to begin. Commonality plays that role for public goods. And the obvious objection — "normies aren't great at seeing unconventional ideas either" — is just the Crossing the Chasm dynamic: visionaries see it early, the majority later. The point is that you *can* appeal directly to the market by simply doing the thing and showing real value, instead of needing a stodgy gatekeeper's permission to even start. As with startups, there's a place here for early backers who see the vision before the majority does — which is exactly what [retroactive funding](./retroactive-funding.md) (#5) rewards.

**5. Proven-but-unpredictable goods.** Domains where you genuinely *can't* pick winners in advance but *can* recognize them afterward, and where attempts are cheap while hits are rare: creative content, open source, journalism, much research. [Retroactive funding](./retroactive-funding.md) is the mechanism that converts "I won't risk funding this" into "I'll reward what demonstrably worked" — and, paired with #4, rewards whoever bet early the way equity rewards seed investors.

**6. Politically-disfavored / suppressible goods.** Advocacy, legal defense, contested speech — things a hostile authority actively wants to *defund*. [Censorship-resistance](../hard-to-stop/censorship-resistance.md) turns "can't" into "can."

**7. Bridging / communication goods.** Funding the spread of depolarizing, noninflammatory content across a divide. Government and charity essentially don't — and couldn't credibly — do this. This is a close cousin of #2, but distinct: #2 funds a concrete *outcome* both sides already agree on, while #7 funds the *communication that reveals or creates* that agreement. It's an input to #2 — the thing that manufactures a hidden majority where the blocker is misunderstanding rather than genuine disagreement.
