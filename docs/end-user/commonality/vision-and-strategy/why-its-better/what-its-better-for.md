# What kinds of public goods is Commonality better for?

We already have governments and charities funding public goods. The world is not devoid of clean water, research, or parks. So the honest question isn't "could a public good exist without Commonality?" — obviously many do. But our existing institutions fail in characteristic ways, and Commonality has mechanisms with different strengths and weaknesses. Which kinds of public goods are *underproduced* today, why, and which of those might Commonality be good for?

## The kinds it's better for

Organized not by topic but by *what's blocking them today*:

**1. Minority-passion goods.** A dedicated minority cares intensely, but can't command a tax-funded majority and never will — niche cultural or scholarly preservation, enthusiast infrastructure, specific community or religious goods, unconventional research. Government structurally *can't* serve these (no majority to approve the spending); charity can, but eats the [free-rider](../credible-solution/assurance-contracts.md) and [discovery](../credible-solution/discovery.md) problems. This is the heartland — and it's not fringe. Most of what people genuinely care about and government ignores lives here. **2. Polarization-trapped goods (hidden majorities).** Broadly wanted, but trapped inside a culture-war bundle so the political system can't deliver them. Commonality's [organic-coalition discovery](./organic-coalitions.md) is purpose-built for this, and essentially nothing else does it: it surfaces the concrete outcome people agree on without forcing them to share a framing. (USER: kind of? Yes, Tally should be good at finding those kinds of coalitions, and Alignment can put funding behind any public-good projects that are aligned with that hidden majority but disfavored by both sides. Actually, come to think of it, LazyGiving's assurance contracts are quite nice for this, specifically because they're capable of aggregating funding from multiple sources: a government (which is either left-wing or right-wing) isn't going to fund something like this; a big charity is probably polarized to one side or the other too; but an assurance contract can get some money from moderately-left-leaning donors and some from moderately-right-leaning donors and that's fine. Retroactive funding, too - some receipts can be bought up by people on the left, some by people on the right, there's no need for a single big org to take the whole thing.)

**3. Sub-jurisdictional / hyper-local goods.** Too small or too specific for government to bother with — this block's park, this town's trail. Denser trust networks and lower pledge thresholds make these [especially natural for Commonality](../so-what/local-government.md). (USER: yes, I do feel like there's something here. Often, especially when living in the city but even when living in the country, I've felt like that. "Here's a tiny thing, only relevant to this one block or whatever. Calling the city to try to get them to handle it feels futile." But part of the point here is that it's the *kind* of thing that is *in theory* the domain of the government - I'm not allowed to just take up a collection among the local people and just get it done. Commonality doesn't actually fix that except maybe as a long-term pie-in-the-sky "let's see if this might someday change the structure of government" kind of thing. But it's true that Commonality *would* be much better suited to this kind of thing than our normal government system is - unlike a government system with a fixed hierarchy of federal -> state -> municipal, Commonality is *much* more capable of coordinating at various scales and across boundaries and so on.)

**4. Orphaned cross-boundary goods.** The mirror image of #3: the beneficiaries don't map onto any *one* jurisdiction, so no government internalizes the full benefit. Inter-town infrastructure, open-source software, internet commons, global health. No single authority has the incentive to fund the whole thing; a permissionless protocol that pools willing payers across boundaries does.

**5. Gatekept / unconventional goods.** Good ideas that don't fit a grant category, come from people without credentials or connections, or are too novel or interdisciplinary to survive a review panel. [Permissionless creation plus crowdsourced filtering](./openness.md) means a project that the committee would have rejected can still find its funders. (USER: Yes. Big Government and Big Charity are both big; big orgs often have trouble with unconventional stuff. In the world of private goods, when there's a good idea that all the big companies are too stodgy to see, someone can make a startup and appeal directly to the market. That's a role that Commonality can play for public goods: you don't need to get a stodgy big org to see your point, you can just appeal directly to people. And, as with startups, there's a place here for early backers who can see the vision - that is, you might object "um, normies aren't great at seeing the value in unconventional ideas either", but that's the whole Crossing the Chasm thing. Visionaries see it early on, the majority later, but at least you *can* appeal directly to the market by simply *doing* it and demonstrating real value, rather than needing to persuade the stodgy gatekeepers in order to be allowed to even start.)

**6. Proven-but-unpredictable goods.** Domains where you genuinely *can't* pick winners in advance but *can* recognize them afterward, and where attempts are cheap while hits are rare: creative content, open source, journalism, much research. [Retroactive funding](./retroactive-funding.md) is the mechanism that converts "I won't risk funding this" into "I'll reward what demonstrably worked." (USER: yes.)

**7. Politically-disfavored / suppressible goods.** Advocacy, legal defense, contested speech — things a hostile authority actively wants to *defund*. [Censorship-resistance](../hard-to-stop/censorship-resistance.md) turns "can't" into "can." (USER: yes.)

**8. Bridging / communication goods.** A genuinely new category: funding the spread of depolarizing, noninflammatory content across a divide. Government and charity essentially don't — and couldn't credibly — do this. (USER: yes, this seems like an example of #2.)
