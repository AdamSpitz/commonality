# The conditional-support problem

On many issues, people won't concede anything unless they're confident the other side is also conceding in good faith. A left-leaning person might think: "If I admit I don't want to defund the police, the right will use my admission as an excuse to ignore actual abuses of police power." And the mirror-image worry exists on the right. Neither side will move first, so nobody moves at all — even when the underlying positions are closer than anyone realizes.

The fix is to structure common-ground statements as **mutual assurances**, so that conceding isn't unilateral. For example, on policing:

- **Left-leaning:** "I'm especially concerned with abuses of police power, but I don't actually want to defund the police, and as long as the right is taking the abuse problem seriously, I'm happy to stand up against the left-wing extremists."
- **Right-leaning:** "I'm primarily concerned with making sure that police can do their job, but I am in favor of punishing police who abuse their power, and as long as the moderate left is pushing back against this defund-the-police nonsense, I'm happy to keep supporting reasonable efforts to punish bad cops."
- **Commonality:** "Obviously police should be accountable when they abuse their power, but also obviously don't defund the police. I've got my own opinions about which is a bigger concern at the moment, but in principle I care about both."

The "as long as you're also..." framing is the key — it makes the commitment **bilateral**. You're not laying down your arms and hoping; you're agreeing to a position *on the condition* that the other side holds up their end.

And the assurance you need is right there in the numbers. You don't need a contract or a promise from anyone: if you can see that 500,000 people from the other side have signed their version of the bilateral statement, that's your confidence that the concession is mutual. The [supporter counts](./why-does-tally-help.md) make the good faith visible.

This structure is what lets the system bridge genuinely hard issues — not by papering over the disagreement, but by giving each side a way to say "I'll meet you here, if you'll meet me here too," and then showing them that the other side already has.
