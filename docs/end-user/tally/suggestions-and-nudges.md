# Suggestions and nudges

Tally can suggest statements you might want to consider. For example: if you signed a very specific statement, it might suggest a clearer or more widely used statement nearby in the implication graph.

These suggestions are called **nudges**. They are meant to help you discover statements you might already believe — not to pressure you into agreeing.

## What a nudge is

A nudge is a suggestion from a service you have chosen to listen to. Each nudge says: "because you signed this statement, you might also want to look at that one."

Nudges can come from different kinds of services:

- an implication-graph nudger that finds related statements
- an explorer that helps you browse a cause area
- a movement-specific mediator, like the CSM mediator, that proposes possible bridge statements

## What nudges are not

- **Not notifications.** They should not interrupt you or demand attention.
- **Not automatic signatures.** Nothing is signed unless you choose to sign it.
- **Not a central recommendation algorithm.** Different nudgers can exist, and you choose which ones to trust.
- **Not proof that you believe something.** A nudge is only an invitation to inspect a statement.

## Your controls

You can ignore any suggestion. You can dismiss suggestions you do not want to see. You can mute a nudger. You can choose which nudgers and attesters you trust in settings.

The important rule is: **your signature is yours.** A suggestion does not count for anything until you explicitly sign the statement.

## Why suggestions are useful

Tally lets people write statements in their own words. That freedom is valuable, but it can make the graph feel fragmented: many people may believe nearly the same thing without using the same sentence. Suggestions help you find nearby wording, broader claims, or related statements that make your position more visible without forcing you to compromise on what you originally wrote.
