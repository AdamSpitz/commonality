# Get your content funded

## What this is

Content Funding lets you get paid directly for the tweets, videos, and posts you already make. It works just like [getting a project funded on LazyGiving](../lazyGiving/get-your-project-funded.md) — supporters pledge, and the money is released only if you reach a funding goal — except the "project" is your content.

You group pieces of content into a *contract*, set a funding goal and a deadline, and supporters pledge toward the individual pieces they value. Reach the goal and the funds are yours. Fall short and everyone is refunded — so there's no downside to asking.

## Why you might want to do this

Ad revenue doesn't pay you for making something good; it pays you for holding attention, which is why the algorithm rewards outrage and clickbait. Paywalls make readers commit before they know your work. Grants mean applications and committees.

Content Funding pays you directly for content people are glad they saw. You can make exactly what you think is worth making — the careful explainer, the thoughtful thread, the video that took three weeks — and let the people who value it fund it. You don't have to optimize for the algorithm, and you don't have to already have a huge following. You have to make something enough people want to reward.

## How it works

**Claim your channel.** Tell us your X account, YouTube channel, or Substack, and prove you control it by posting a short verification code we give you — a tweet, a line in a video description, or a post. That links the channel to you, so funds addressed to your content come to you and nobody else.

You can browse your funded content before doing that. If someone sends you a claim page, you should be able to see what was funded, how much is waiting, and why people funded it before you connect or create any wallet.

**Group content into a contract and set a goal.** Pick the pieces you want to fund together — a month of videos, a series of posts, a single big piece — and set how much you're asking for and a deadline. Each piece becomes something supporters can pledge toward individually.

**Or fund future content.** If the pieces do not exist yet, create a future-content round instead: describe what you plan to make, set a goal and deadline, and let supporters pledge before you do the work. Supporters receive non-transferable receipts for backing the round. Later, when you publish the actual posts/videos/articles, you attach them to the round and the backers can claim normal transferable tokens for each finished piece.

**Supporters pledge.** People who value your work pledge toward the pieces they like, or toward the future-content round they want you to make. Their money is held safely and only moves if your contract reaches its goal by the deadline; otherwise everyone is refunded. When you hit the goal, the funds are released to you.

**You can be discovered, not just found.** Because a content contract is a LazyGiving project, it plugs into [Alignment](../alignment/index.md): supporters who've pledged to a cause — or [delegates](../shared/key-ideas/delegation.md) funding on their behalf — can route money to your content because it serves something they care about. This is also how funding can target *kinds* of content: a cause pool dedicated to, say, [writing that informs rather than inflames](../civility/index.md) can fund yours when it qualifies.

**Someone may have started without you.** Anyone can create a contract for your content before you've claimed your channel, to put money behind work they want to reward. The funds wait for you; claiming your channel is how you collect them. The third party never receives your money.

## If someone already funded your work

The first message should be simple: **people pooled money because they liked your work.** You do not need to understand smart contracts to inspect that offer.

A claim page should show you:

- which content items were funded;
- how much money is waiting;
- what supporters or trusted evaluators said about why the work was valuable;
- who started the contract, if it was fan-created;
- what you need to do to claim the funds.

You should not have to connect a wallet just to look around. The wallet step belongs at the moment you choose to claim money. If you do not already have a crypto wallet, the app can create a managed wallet behind the scenes; your practical experience should be “sign in, verify that this is my channel, withdraw.”

Fan-created contracts are allowed because they make the system work before every creator has heard of it. A supporter can say, “I want this thread/video/post rewarded,” start a contract, and share the claim link with you. If the contract succeeds before you arrive, the funds wait in a channel escrow until you prove the channel is yours.

## Claiming, control, and safety

Claiming your channel does two things:

1. it lets you withdraw funds that are already waiting for you; and
2. it tells future contracts where your payouts should go.

Claiming does **not** force you to actively manage the channel. Fans can keep creating contracts for your content unless you later choose to take full channel control.

Full channel control is optional. If you turn it on, only you can create new contracts for your content. This is useful if you want to manage pricing, goals, and which pieces are grouped together yourself.

There is also a safety valve for contracts created before you took control. If someone set unreasonable terms for your work — for example, pricing it absurdly low — taking control can give you a limited veto window. Vetoing a pre-control contract cancels it, refunds supporters, and frees the content so you can start over on better terms. The point is to let fans bootstrap funding without letting strangers permanently lock you into terms you never accepted.

## Getting started

Open the **Creators** section and find your platform (X, YouTube, Substack). Search for your channel. If it's already there — because someone funded your content before you arrived — claim it. If not, register it. Once it's claimed, create a contract: add already-published content, or choose a future-content round if you are asking people to fund work you have not published yet. Set your goal and deadline, then share it with your audience.

You don't have to build an audience here first. The point is to get paid for the work — so make the thing you think is worth making, and let the people who agree fund it.
