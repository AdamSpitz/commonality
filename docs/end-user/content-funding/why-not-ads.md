# Why fund content this way? (The case against ads)

Social-media content is a public good, and almost the only way we've ever found to make people "pay" for it is ads. That's not an accident. Once a post is published it's *non-excludable* — anyone can read it, and there's no practical way to charge per view. Micropayments were supposed to fix that, but they don't work: nobody wants to decide whether a tweet is worth a tenth of a cent before reading it. So we fall back on ads, which at least let advertisers foot the bill in exchange for attention.

The trouble is that ads pay for attention, not value. They reward whatever keeps people scrolling — outrage, clickbait, engagement bait — not the content people are actually glad they saw. And everyone finds them annoying.

## "Why not just put up a paywall?"

The other way to make content excludable is to charge for access: subscriptions and paywalls. These genuinely work — Substack, paywalled newsrooms, members-only posts — but they fix the funding problem by breaking the thing that makes content valuable in the first place. A paywalled post can't be linked, quoted, or stumbled upon; it's walled off from exactly the people a healthier information commons most needs to reach. And readers end up juggling a dozen separate subscriptions to follow the handful of writers they like in each, so most just don't.

Content funding keeps the content open to everyone while still paying the creator. The post stays free to read and free to share; the people who valued it pay for it; nobody is locked out for lack of a subscription.

## "Why not government or big charity?"

If you take ads away without a paywall, you're back to the public-goods problem: how does content that doesn't pay for itself get funded? The usual answers are government or big charity, and neither is a good fit for social-media content. Both are too capturable and too coarse-grained to decide which posts and videos deserve support — and you really don't want either of them in that role for something as politically charged as the public conversation.

## So: a better way to crowdfund public goods

The real question is whether there's a *better* way to crowdfund public goods — especially fine-grained, sometimes-controversial ones like social-media content. That's exactly what LazyGiving is built to be. Funding flows directly from the people who value a piece to the creator who made it; nobody can stop you from supporting what you want, and nobody can make you support what you don't. The infrastructure is neutral, so there's no central gatekeeper to capture.

LazyGiving beats the alternatives on their own terms:

- **Better than government or big charity:** it's fine-grained instead of coarse, and it routes money by what individual supporters actually value rather than by what a committee or grant program approves.
- **Better than Patreon-style tipping:** it combines mechanisms that ordinary tipping lacks — and those mechanisms are what actually make it work.

### Two mechanisms, two jobs

[Assurance contracts](../lazyGiving/assurance-contracts.md) and [retroactive funding](../lazyGiving/retroactive-funding.md) answer two different questions, and content funding needs both.

- **Assurance contracts answer "does this get made at all?"** A creator may not be willing to put in the effort without some guarantee they'll be paid. An assurance contract gives them that: supporters pledge, and the money is released only if the goal is reached — so the "I'll pay if enough others do" coordination problem is solved up front, before the work happens.

- **Retroactive funding answers "who fronts the money?"** The people who put up the early funding don't have to be the people who most want this kind of content to exist. They can be people who are simply good at *predicting* that a given creator will deliver — and who expect to recoup their money (at a profit) by selling to later supporters once the work has proven itself. Even when the early funders *are* true supporters, retroactive funding lets them put in more than they'd personally be willing to pay just to make the content exist, because they expect to be bought out later. Either way, more money shows up earlier than the creator's most devoted fans could supply on their own.

[Delegation](../shared/key-ideas/delegation.md) rounds this out: you can back someone whose taste you trust instead of judging every piece yourself.

None of these ideas is brand new on its own — assurance contracts are old; retroactive funding is recent — but the *combination* is what makes content funding work. It gives us a way to pay directly for the content we actually want, with no advertiser in the middle, and to do it at the fine grain social media requires.

## "Even if this works, the ads are still there"

That's true, and it's worth being clear-eyed about. Getting rid of ads is really two problems: first, build a working alternative; second, actually move off the ad-funded system. You can't do the second without the first — so building the alternative comes first regardless.

And building it is worth doing on its own merits, well before anyone "switches off" anything. Plenty of content is already underfunded by the ad model: there are people who'll see the value of, say, the [noninflammatory political content](../shared/use-case-walkthroughs/noninflammatory-content.md) we're aiming for, and be glad to help fund it — content the engagement-driven ad model actively punishes. Content funding doesn't need ads to disappear to be useful. It just needs to fund the good content the current system won't. Once that alternative exists, turning the ads off somewhere stops meaning the good content dies with them.
