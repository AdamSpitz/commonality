# Content Funding

Pay creators directly for the social-media content you value — the tweets and threads on X, the videos on YouTube, the posts on Substack. (We're not affiliated with any of those platforms.)

Under the hood, Content Funding is [LazyGiving](../lazyGiving/index.md) pointed at content: supporters pledge, and the money moves only when the conditions are met — otherwise everyone is refunded. But the simplest way to understand the site is the three things you can actually do.

## Three ways to fund content

- **Reward a post you loved.** You read a thread, watched a video, or read an essay that genuinely helped you. Put money behind *that specific piece* — not just a like. Supporters pool funds on the work, and the creator claims them, even a creator who's never heard of us yet. The content already exists, so there's nothing to wait for: you're rewarding good work that already happened.
- **Commission a creator's next chapter.** Pledge toward a creator's *future* work — a month of videos, a series of posts — as an [assurance contract](../lazyGiving/assurance-contracts.md): "I'll chip in $5 *as long as* enough others do too." Reach the goal and the funds are released; fall short and everyone is refunded. The creator gets a guarantee before they start the work, and nobody risks anything.
- **Fund a whole *kind* of content.** Pledge toward a *type* of content you want more of — like [writing that informs rather than inflames](../shared/use-case-walkthroughs/noninflammatory-content.md) — and let it fund qualifying work, old or new. This runs on cause pools over on [Alignment](../alignment/index.md), with an [evaluator](../alignment/ai-evaluators.md) deciding what counts; the [Civility](../civility/index.md) vertical is built this way.

The first two are about *specific* work — a piece that exists, or a creator's next output. The third is about a *category*, and it feeds the first two: a cause pool can reward existing pieces that qualify or commission new ones.

## How it works underneath

A content contract isn't a special new thing — it's an ordinary [LazyGiving assurance contract](../lazyGiving/assurance-contracts.md), organized so that the "project" is your content. The creator owns the contract and receives the money; anyone can start one *for* a creator who hasn't shown up yet, but the funds wait for the creator, who [claims them by proving they control the account](get-your-content-funded.md) — never the third party.

Everything else LazyGiving offers — [delegation](../shared/key-ideas/delegation.md) (let someone you trust do the picking) and [retroactive funding](../lazyGiving/retroactive-funding.md) (early funders who expect to be bought out later) — comes along too, because a content contract *is* a LazyGiving project.

## Why fund content this way?

Social-media content is a public good, and right now we pay for it almost entirely with ads. Ads reward whatever holds attention — which in practice means outrage, clickbait, and engagement bait — not the content people are actually glad they saw. Content Funding is a way to pay creators directly for work people value, without an advertiser in the middle deciding what gets made.

And because content contracts plug into the rest of the ecosystem, you can fund more than specific pieces. Through [Alignment](../alignment/index.md), pooled cause-money can flow to whole *kinds* of content that the ad model has never rewarded well: content that's funny, insightful, carefully researched, or [written to inform rather than inflame](../shared/use-case-walkthroughs/noninflammatory-content.md). If LazyGiving is a good way to fund public goods, this is what it looks like aimed at the public good of a healthier information commons.
