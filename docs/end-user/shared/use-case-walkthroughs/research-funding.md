# Getting a research project funded

> **Status: this walkthrough is functional but not yet compelling.** The core mechanics are correct, but the specific story vehicle (niche research) may not be the most intuitive way to introduce retroactive funding. Revisit later and consider whether a different vehicle (community documentary? investigative reporting? open-source software?) would make the "reward early believers" concept land harder.

A scientist has an idea. It's too niche for traditional grants — no committee is going to bet on it. It's not flashy enough for crowdfunding. But a handful of people who understand the field can see that it might work.

Here's what happens.


## Early believers

A delegate — someone who knows the field and has earned the trust of a small group of donors — spots the project. She's directed pooled funds toward this area before, and her track record is transparent: people can see what she's funded and how it turned out.

She decides this project is worth backing. She directs some of her delegated funds toward it, and a few individual donors buy early tokens. Nobody else has to agree yet. Nobody has to evaluate the proposal against a rubric. The people who understand the field made a judgment call.

The project gets enough early funding to get off the ground.


## The project delivers

Six months later, the project publishes results. They're real: verifiable, significant, the kind of thing that makes people in the field say "I wish I'd funded that." The results are public, the track record is on-chain, and anyone can see exactly what was accomplished.


## Retroactive funding

Now comes the part that makes the system work long-term.

You're a donor who cares about this field but doesn't want to gamble on unknowns. You don't have the expertise to evaluate early-stage proposals. That's fine — you don't have to. You can look at projects that have *already delivered results* and fund those.

When you buy tokens in a project that already succeeded, part of what you pay flows back to the people who believed in it early — the delegate who spotted it, the donors who took the risk when nobody else would. That's their reward for good judgment.

And that reward creates the incentive for *more* people to develop good judgment. The next time a niche project appears, there are more scouts looking for it, because scouting pays off.


## The cycle

1. **Early believers** fund projects based on judgment and expertise.
2. **Projects deliver** verifiable results.
3. **Retroactive funders** fund proven successes — no gambling required.
4. **Early believers get rewarded**, incentivizing more early-stage scouting.
5. Repeat.

This is a nano-VC system for public goods. The early backer took a risk when nobody else believed in the project. When you come along later and fund it, part of your money goes to them as thanks for that bet. That's it — no jargon, no complex financial instruments. Just: reward the people who were right early.


## What this illustrates

**Delegation lets experts lead.** You don't need a grant committee. A few people who understand the field can direct pooled funds, and their track record speaks for itself.

**Retroactive funding removes the need to gamble.** Most donors don't want to evaluate early-stage proposals. They don't have to. They can wait for results and fund what worked.

**Rewarding early believers creates a virtuous cycle.** The financial reward for good judgment attracts more people with good judgment, which means more good projects get early funding.
