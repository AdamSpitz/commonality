# Getting a research project funded

> **Status: this walkthrough is functional but not yet compelling.** The core mechanics are correct, but the specific story vehicle (niche research) may not be the most intuitive way to introduce retroactive funding. Revisit later and consider whether a different vehicle (community documentary? investigative reporting? open-source software?) would make the "reimburse early contributors" concept land harder.

A scientist has an idea. It's too niche for traditional grants — no committee is going to back it. It's not flashy enough for crowdfunding. But a handful of people who understand the field can see that it might work.

Here's what happens.

## Early contributors

A delegate — someone who knows the field and has earned the trust of a small group of donors — spots the project. She's directed pooled funds toward this area before, and her track record is transparent: people can see what she's funded and how it turned out.

She decides this project is worth backing. She directs some of her delegated funds toward it, and a few individual donors contribute early. Their non-transferable receipts record what they contributed and preserve an at-cost reimbursement claim if the project succeeds. Nobody else has to agree yet. The people who understand the field make a judgment call.

The project gets enough early funding to get off the ground.

## The project delivers

Six months later, the project publishes results. They're real: verifiable, significant, the kind of thing that makes people in the field say "I wish I'd funded that." The results are public, the track record is on-chain, and anyone can see exactly what was accomplished.

## Retroactive funding

Now comes the part that makes the system work long-term.

You're a donor who cares about this field but doesn't want to gamble on unknowns. You don't have the expertise to evaluate early-stage proposals. That's fine — you don't have to. You can look at projects that have *already delivered results* and fund those.

Your donation enters the project's reimbursement waterfall. It returns early contributors' money pro-rata, never more than each person originally contributed. Their receipts are recognition records, not assets to trade, and nobody receives interest, a premium, or a profit.

Reimbursed contributors can use that same giving budget to scout and fund the next promising project. Their public record — what they backed and what later succeeded — helps donors decide whom to trust with delegated budgets.

## The cycle

1. **Early contributors** fund projects based on judgment and expertise.
2. **Projects deliver** verifiable results.
3. **Later donors** fund proven successes without predicting winners.
4. **Early contributors get their money back**, pro-rata and at cost, so they can fund the next project.
5. Their track records help good scouts attract larger delegated budgets.
6. Repeat.

The point is capital velocity, not financial return: get your contribution back and fund the next one.

## What this illustrates

**Delegation lets experts lead.** You don't need a grant committee. A few people who understand the field can direct pooled funds, and their track record speaks for itself.

**Retroactive funding removes the need to gamble.** Most donors don't want to evaluate early-stage proposals. They don't have to. They can wait for results and fund what worked.

**Reimbursement sustains early funding.** Returning early contributors' money at cost lets the same giving budget support another attempt, while public track records help capable scouts earn trust and delegated responsibility.
