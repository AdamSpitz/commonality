# Walkthrough: three towns fund shared water infrastructure without the province

This walks through a longer-term scenario showing how Commonality's mechanisms can shift public-goods funding from the provincial level to the local level — not through confrontation, but by routing around.

## The situation

Three small towns in the same region — Millbrook, Cedarville, and Harton — all have aging water treatment infrastructure. Each town needs roughly $800K in upgrades over the next five years.

In the traditional world, each town would independently lobby the province for infrastructure grants. The province would evaluate applications, attach conditions (compliance requirements, mandated contractors, reporting obligations), and eventually fund some of them — maybe in three years, maybe never. The towns compete against each other and against every other municipality in the province for a limited pot.

Here's what happens with Commonality.

## Phase 1: each town builds local capacity (year 1)

This phase looks a lot like the [defunding walkthrough](./defunding.md). Each town independently adopts Commonality for small local projects:

  - Millbrook already has infrastructure from the mentorship program situation — delegation networks, pledge capacity, a track record.
  - Cedarville's volunteer fire department sets up a supplementary funding contract. A retired engineer, Ravi, becomes a delegate for infrastructure projects. Forty families route $30/month through him.
  - Harton's community association uses Commonality for a parks maintenance fund. A local contractor, Diane, attests alignment for projects she considers well-scoped.

None of this is about water infrastructure yet. Each town is just building the muscles — delegation networks, pledge habits, track records — on easy, uncontroversial projects.

## Phase 2: the towns find each other

  - A resident of Millbrook creates a statement: "Our region's water infrastructure needs upgrading and we shouldn't have to wait for the province."
  - An implication attester (AI service) notices that people in Cedarville and Harton have signed similar statements — "Cedarville needs water treatment upgrades," "rural communities deserve reliable water infrastructure." It connects these through the implication graph.
  - Now supporters in all three towns can see each other's statements and pledges in their funding portals. Nobody organized this. The system discovered the shared interest.

Ravi in Cedarville sees Millbrook's water-related pledges. Diane in Harton sees both. They start talking — not because an institution connected them, but because the implication graph made the overlap visible.

## Phase 3: the shared assurance contract

The three towns set up a shared assurance contract: "Fund a regional water infrastructure upgrade — $2.4M over 5 years, split across three towns."

  - Monthly pledge threshold: $40K/month across all three towns.
  - Each town's delegates direct funds toward their town's specific projects. Ravi handles Cedarville's scope. Diane handles Harton's. Maria (from the defunding walkthrough) handles Millbrook's.
  - Pledges come from all three towns. Some residents pledge directly; many route funds through their local delegate. A few businesses contribute through bridge operators for tax deductibility.

Within two months, pledges reach $35K/month — not quite the threshold, but visible and growing.

## Phase 4: the province notices

The province is aware that three municipalities are publicly building independent funding for infrastructure it considers its domain. A few things happen:

  - **The province can't easily suppress it.** It's voluntary crowdfunding for public infrastructure. Trying to ban it is politically toxic — "the province is stopping towns from fixing their own water."
  - **The province's leverage weakens.** Normally the province would attach conditions to infrastructure grants — maybe requiring the towns to consolidate their water systems under a provincial authority, or mandating specific contractors. But the towns now have a visible alternative. The conditions become negotiable.
  - **Other towns are watching.** The assurance contract and its progress are onchain. Five more municipalities in the region are paying attention.

The province offers the three towns an unconditional infrastructure grant — no consolidation requirement, no mandated contractors — to "supplement community efforts." This is a win: the province is now competing with the towns' own funding capacity rather than dictating terms from above.

## Phase 5: the new normal (year 3+)

  - The three towns accept the provincial grant but keep their Commonality infrastructure running. The assurance contract drops to a lower threshold (covering the gap between the grant and the full cost) and continues funding the work.
  - Ravi and Diane's track records — transparent, onchain, verifiable — attract pledges from residents of other towns who want similar competence directing their infrastructure funds.
  - Two of the five watching municipalities set up their own infrastructure contracts. They copy the playbook openly.
  - At the next municipal election, a Cedarville council candidate runs on "keep more tax dollars local" — pointing to the demonstrated success of community-funded infrastructure as evidence that the province's coordination role isn't worth the strings attached.

## What this illustrates

  - **Horizontal coalitions bypass the higher level.** The three towns coordinated directly — pooled pledges, shared expertise, split scope — without the province acting as intermediary. The province's traditional role as coordination layer was replaced by a protocol.
  - **Local delegates did the coordination.** Ravi, Diane, and Maria aren't provincial bureaucrats. They're local people trusted by their neighbors, whose track records are transparent. The delegation system provided accountability without hierarchy.
  - **The ratchet worked.** Each town's capacity from Phase 1 (built for unrelated projects) carried over to Phase 3. The infrastructure didn't degrade between uses — it accumulated.
  - **The province moderated rather than escalated.** Faced with visible community capacity and political cost, the province offered better terms. The credible threat shifted the negotiation without anyone spending a confrontational dollar.
  - **Contagion is natural.** The playbook is public. Other towns replicated it without anyone organizing a movement.
  - **The tax-competition argument emerged organically.** Nobody started with "we should devolve tax revenue." They started with "let's fix our water." But the demonstrated success of local funding created a natural political argument for keeping tax dollars local — not as ideology, but as pragmatism.
