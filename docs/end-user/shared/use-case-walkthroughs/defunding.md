# Walkthrough: a town's youth mentorship program gets defunded

This walks through a concrete scenario end-to-end, showing how the various pieces of Commonality fit together in practice.

## The situation

Millbrook is a small town whose youth mentorship program has been funded by a provincial grant for eight years. The provincial government, now controlled by a different party, announces that the grant will be cut next fiscal year unless the school board adopts curriculum changes the community doesn't want.

In the traditional world, the community's options are: comply, lobby the province, wait for the next election, or let the program die. All of these are slow, uncertain, or humiliating.

Here's what happens with Commonality.

## Phase 1: Get on the rails (months before the crisis)

A local community leader, Maria, hears about Commonality and decides to try it for the mentorship program — not because she's worried about defunding (not yet), but because the [infrastructure](../../commonality/vision-and-strategy/ease-of-adoption/rails.md) is just better than the ad hoc donation tracking they've been doing.

  - She creates a **statement**: "Millbrook's youth mentorship program deserves community support."
  - She signs it. A dozen other community members sign it too. No coordination needed — each person just signs individually.
  - She sets up a simple **assurance contract** for supplementary donations to the program: $500 threshold, 6-month deadline. Local families pledge $10-20 each. It's basically a [tip jar](../../commonality/vision-and-strategy/ease-of-adoption/tip-jar-upgrade-path.md) — small amounts, low stakes — but donors get verifiable receipts and leaderboard credit.
  - The program's existing charity accepts donations through a [bridge operator](../../commonality/vision-and-strategy/ease-of-adoption/bridges.md), so tradfi donors can contribute without touching crypto. Tax receipts still work normally.

At this point, Commonality is doing almost nothing that the community wasn't already doing. It's just recording it on better infrastructure.

## Phase 2: Build the network (still before the crisis)

  - Maria becomes a **delegate**. A few families who trust her judgment route delegatable notes through her — "spend this on whatever you think best for Millbrook youth programs." She starts with $2K in delegated funds. Small potatoes, but her track record is now onchain.
  - A retired teacher, James, starts **attesting alignment** for projects he thinks serve the mentorship mission. He's the sole trusted attester for now — functionally identical to the old "the board decides what fits our mission" process, just recorded transparently.
  - An **implication attester** (AI service) notices that "Millbrook's youth mentorship program deserves community support" implies a broader statement — "rural communities should be able to fund their own youth programs" — that 300 people in other towns have already signed. Millbrook's supporters are now connected to a wider network without having done anything.

## Phase 3: The credible threat

The province announces the funding cut. Now things get serious.

  - Maria sets up a **standby assurance contract**: "If provincial funding is cut, fund the mentorship program at $120K/year." Monthly pledges, $10K/month threshold.
  - 180 families pledge an average of $60/month. That's $10,800/month in publicly verifiable, locked pledges — past the threshold, but the community keeps going to build a buffer.
  - A local business owner pledges $2K/month through a bridge operator (tax-deductible via the program's existing charity).
  - The wider network kicks in: three families from neighboring towns who signed the broader "rural communities" statement see Millbrook's project in their cause board (via the implication attestation) and pledge $50/month each.

The pledges are onchain. The province can see exactly how much is committed and by whom. This isn't a petition with signatures — it's $13K/month in conditionally locked money. The community is demonstrating that the program survives without provincial funding. This is the [credible threat](../../commonality/vision-and-strategy/hard-to-stop/credible-threat.md).

## Phase 4a: The threat works

The province, facing similar credible threats from several communities, quietly drops the curriculum conditions from the grant. The assurance contract doesn't activate. All pledges are refunded. Total cost to the community: zero.

But the infrastructure stays in place. The delegation network exists. The track record is onchain. Next time the province tries this, the credible threat can be re-established in days rather than months.

## Phase 4b: The threat is called

Alternatively: the province follows through on the cut. The assurance contract activates.

  - The $10K/month threshold is met (it grew to $11.5K during the standoff as more people pledged). Monthly funding flows to the program.
  - Maria, as delegate, directs the supplementary delegated funds ($2K, now grown to $5K as the crisis attracted more delegators) toward specific needs — new mentoring materials, a part-time coordinator.
  - James attests alignment for a neighboring town's similar program, which now shows up in Millbrook's cause board. A donor who pledged for Millbrook sees the neighboring program and pledges there too.
  - The program's track record — months of successful delivery, transparent spending, growing community support — is all onchain. When the program applies for a private foundation grant six months later, the application includes verifiable proof of community backing. The foundation, impressed by the evidence, provides matching funds.

## What this illustrates

  - **Every step was individually useful.** Maria didn't need to plan for the crisis. Getting on the rails was useful on its own. Building a delegation network was useful on its own. The crisis preparation was costless (pledges are refundable).
  - **No coordination was required.** Each family pledged individually. Maria became a delegate on her own. James attested alignment on his own. The wider network connected automatically through implication attestations.
  - **The system scaled from tip jar to serious funding.** The same infrastructure that handled $500 in supplementary donations handled $120K/year in replacement funding.
  - **The credible threat might have been enough.** The most valuable outcome — the province backing down — happened without anyone spending a dollar. The [after-tax disadvantage](../../commonality/vision-and-strategy/hard-to-stop/after-tax.md) was irrelevant because the money was never spent.
  - **Traditional finance coexisted throughout.** The charity still processed tax-deductible donations. The bridge operator handled credit card payments. The onchain record was the authoritative one, but nobody had to go "full crypto."
