Speaking the truth in love

Jesus told you to do a hard thing. The internet pays people to do the opposite.

Make your case to people who disagree with you — with gentleness and respect, so they can actually hear it instead of writing you off. That's not a tactic. It's an instruction, repeated all through the New Testament.

And right now nothing rewards it. The feeds pay for outrage and dunks, so the loudest, angriest version of every faith is the one that travels. Koinonia funds the other thing — the patient, winsome, hard-to-argue-with witness — and hands you a simple way to back the people doing it.

"Always be prepared to give a reason for the hope that is in you — yet do this with gentleness and respect."1 Peter 3:15

Why this is the work "Wait, is this crypto stuff?"
You already believe this part

So much of the New Testament is instructions for getting along with people you disagree with.

Not agreeing with them — getting along with them, and reaching them, while you still disagree. You don't have to be talked into the disposition this funds. You're already under orders to cultivate it.

Matthew 5:9
"Blessed are the peacemakers, for they shall be called children of God."

Not peace-keepers. Peace-makers — the ones who go into a divide and build something across it.

James 1:19
"Let everyone be quick to listen, slow to speak, slow to anger."

"Listen and engage," written two thousand years early. It's the whole posture in nine words.

Ephesians 4:15
"Speaking the truth in love."

Not truth without love (that's cruelty). Not love without truth (that's flattery). Both at once — the hard part.

Colossians 4:6
"Let your speech be gracious, seasoned with salt, so you may know how to answer each person."

Tailor the delivery to the listener. That's exactly what a good noninflammatory filter does.

Romans 12:18, 21
"As far as it depends on you, live at peace with everyone… overcome evil with good."

Relentless goodwill aimed at the people who are hardest to bear. The ask doesn't get more demanding than this.

Acts 17:22–28
Paul at the Areopagus: he credits what the Athenians got right, quotes their own poets, then makes his case.

The most hostile forum in the ancient world, and he doesn't call them stupid. The noninflammatory move, modeled by the apostle himself.

"Listen and engage" is the civic version. "Love them" is the bigger thing — and it includes telling people hard truths.
So don't hear this as "tone it down, never make anyone uncomfortable." Loving someone means being willing to say the hard thing ("faithful are the wounds of a friend"). The catch is that a hard truth delivered as an insult never lands — the listener just hears "you think I'm stupid and evil" and tunes out. Koinonia doesn't soften your message. It funds the delivery that gets it through the door, so the truth you're carrying can actually be heard. You still supply the truth.
What this actually is

A Christian front door to the same engine that funds civil, persuasive content.

Not a new protocol bolted onto your faith. The same crowdfunding rails and open, swappable AI evaluators that power the broader effort — with framing, examples, and a community that already understands why.

01 · The work

Fund a witness people will actually read

Writers and creators who make the Christian case thoughtfully enough that skeptics read it instead of tuning out — the Areopagus, crowdfunded. There's a large audience open to this, and almost no one paying the people who can reach them.

→ The reward that doesn't exist yet.

02 · The bridge

Even skeptics can chip in

Because the content is built to be heard rather than to insult, secular moderates can co-fund the very same pieces. That co-funding is a small act of peacemaking across the exact divide — a bridge built with money before a word is exchanged.

→ Loving your neighbor, by the wallet.

03 · Hand off the picking

Let someone you trust choose

Don't want to vet every piece? Delegate your giving to a pastor, an elder, a friend whose judgment you trust — and take it back anytime. Pledge $10 a month, point it at someone wise, and never think about it again.

→ The way your church already works.

Why it fits the church specifically

This isn't a generic giving app pointed at Christians.

Several things about how the church already works make these rails fit it better than almost any other community on earth.

You already trust the right people

Every congregation already has the network this needs: a pastor, elders, deacons you'd actually trust to direct a gift wisely. Hand them that authority directly — and take it back anytime — instead of mailing a check to a distant office and hoping.

By their fruits you shall know them

You don't have to gamble on a glossy proposal. Fund the ministry that has already borne fruit. The people who spotted it early and took the risk get repaid by those who fund it after it's proven. The talents get invested, not buried.

Keep doing what you're doing

You already give generously, mostly through the church. These are rails for that, not a replacement — the bookkeeping is handled, the ledger is honest and open. Nobody's asked to stop tithing and start trading anything.

No bank to freeze, no platform to ban

When a ministry gets quietly dropped by a payment processor, the question isn't "is there a better system?" — it's "is there any system?" These rails can't be deplatformed, because there's no central account to cut off.

And then, the bigger thing

Once it exists, churches can act together — without merging.

This is the second reason to build it, not the first. But it's real: your congregation can rally around a need in a single Sunday, yet ten churches that agree on almost everything still can't fund one thing together — not without a committee, a denomination, a treasurer, and a fight about who's in charge.

You don't have to merge the rooms to act together in the hall.
Fifty churches across three denominations want the same crisis-pregnancy center, disaster response, or Bible translation funded. They pool their gifts toward that one work, then go their separate ways. No permanent org, no one in charge, no doctrinal treaty. The tool funds what you already agree on and leaves the rest alone.

The honest part

"Isn't this the weird crypto/AI stuff I'm skeptical of?"

Fair question. Yes — underneath, this runs on the same technology as cryptocurrency, and it uses AI to do the legwork of reading mountains of content and surfacing the rare good piece. But you'd never have to touch any of that. The plan is for it to feel like any normal giving app: you'd see causes and creators, see what they've done, give with a tap, get a receipt. The blockchain part is just why the books can't be cooked and the account can't be frozen. The AI part is just why you don't have to wade through the slop yourself — and its instructions are open for anyone to read, and you can pick whose judgment to trust or ignore it entirely.

Three honest caveats: (1) the smooth, non-scary version described here still needs to be built — this is what it will feel like, not what ships today. (2) A real Christian should build and voice this; an outsider ventriloquizing the faith would ring false. (3) Don't over-promise the culture war — funding civil, persuasive witness clearly clears the bar; reshaping the wider secular culture is a genuine open question worth being humble about.

"If it is possible, as far as it depends on you, live at peace with everyone."
Romans 12:18 — the whole point, in one line. This is just rails for the doing of it.
