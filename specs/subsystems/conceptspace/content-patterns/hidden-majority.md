# Hidden majorities

Commonality has the ability to **reveal hidden majorities** that the current polarized (e.g. left/right) coalition structure obscures.

The pattern: on many polarized issues, the two "sides" that dominate public discourse are actually minority positions. There's a majority, probably even a supermajority, that holds an ordinary common-sense view — but nobody knows it, because the current system forces everyone to pick a team, and each team's position is defined by its loudest members. The implication graph can make these hidden majorities visible.

For each such issue, we're expecting content to be (roughly) classifiable into categories like:
- **Pole statements** — the positions held by the crazies, who are loud and so the two parties rally around those positions
- **Normal-people-from-each-side statements** — what most people on each "side" actually think
- **A commonality statement** — the position that both normal-positions imply, which is probably the supermajority position if anyone bothered to ask

The point isn't that "common sense" / "moderate" / "compromise" positions are always right. The point is that when a supermajority position *exists* but is *invisible* because the political system is structured around two coalitions that (a) bundle it with other things, and (b) teach their side that the other side is full of nutty extremists who wouldn't support this obvious common-sense position... that's a bug the system fixes. Let's at least have a system that allows us to *notice* that this commonality statement actually has quite a lot of support from people who we thought were on opposing sides.

## Sub-patterns

I think there are several different kinds of patterns like this.

### There's a reasonable compromise in the middle

Sometimes there's an issue where the two sides genuinely have different preferences, but there's a middle ground that the moderates on each side would prefer to fighting.

Note that the natural phrasing for each category of statement is different:
  - The moderate-from-each-side statements is probably "I'd prefer..." These are the statements that the moderates of each side actually believe; they just happen to be common-sense positions that have reasonable overlap with the other side's moderate position.
  - Whereas the natural phrasing for the commonality statement is probably more like "I'd be okay with it if..." These aren't necessarily the statements that each side's moderates would *prefer* if they could have everything they wanted, but there's enough overlap that they'd be fine with it.

#### Examples

##### Abortion
  - Moderate left might prefer for abortion to be available until *at least* 12 weeks.
  - Moderate right might prefer for abortion to be available until *at most* 16 weeks.
  - So the compromise is 12-16 weeks, which isn't exactly either side's ideal but it's fine with the moderates on both sides.

##### Immigration

  - Moderate left might prefer to deport only the illegal immigrants who are criminals in some other way as well, and leave peaceful otherwise-law-abiding illegal immigrants alone.
  - Moderate right might prefer to deport all illegal aliens, though obviously the criminal ones are a higher priority.
  - So the compromise might be "I want the criminal-in-some-other-way-too ones to be deported, I can accept it if some peaceful ones get deported too, I can accept it if some peaceful ones don't get deported."

### No major controversy among normal people, just nutty extremists on one or both sides

Unlike the "compromise in the middle" pattern, here we've just got a vocal minority (sometimes one on each side, sometimes just one on one side) creating the illusion of a controversy that barely exists. Most people on both sides actually agree, but the loud fringe gets disproportionate attention, making it seem like "that side" disagrees. The commonality statement here isn't a compromise; it's just the obvious consensus that almost everyone already holds but nobody realizes is shared.

e.g.
  - Police:
    - Extreme leftists overemphasize/exaggerate/invent examples of the police abusing their power and they say "defund the police".
      - They get covered heavily by the media, and so the right thinks "the left wants to defund the police" and opposes even accountability reforms by association.
    - So what I want is for moderate leftists to have a way to say "for crying out loud, no, obviously don't defund the police; these idiot extremists don't speak for us". But they also *don't* want to come across as defending abuses of power by the police. (Part of the point here is that the sane people need to have a way to push back against the nutty extremists on their side, but without feeling like they're betraying their side.) So the moderate leftists sign a statement that *both* pushes back against the extreme leftists but also emphasizes that they're against abuses of police power. "Obviously police should be accountable when they abuse their power, but also obviously don't defund the police."
    - That's a statement the right can agree with too.

Like... this isn't really "reasonable compromise in the middle" - there's barely any disagreement at all.

This doesn't mean there are no differences between the left-wing and right-wing moderates. But often it's differences in emphasis as a result of differences in temperament. (e.g. In general: the right defends the just parts of the social order; the left attacks the unjust parts of the social order. So left-leaning people in general do tend to be more inclined to focus on "hey, that policeman abused his power", whereas right-leaning people tend to focus more on "make sure the police can do their job." There are still meaningful differences between left-leaning normal people and right-leaning normal people, but the difference isn't necessarily that they have different values or even different preferred policies, just different temperaments giving them different inclinations regarding what to focus on.)

I wonder whether it might be useful/important to have some way of making people's support for these statements *conditional* on the other side agreeing too. (I dunno whether this means some sort of explicit mechanism like an assurance contract - I'm not sure how that would work. But even just writing it into the language of the commonality statement might be useful and important.) That is: I can imagine the left-leaning people being unwilling to sign the commonality statement because they're like, "Look, in principle, of course I don't want to defund the police. But if I admit that, I know that you right-wing assholes are going to use my admission as an excuse to go ahead and ignore the actual abuses of police power that do happen." And the right-leaning people are similarly going to same, "Look, in principle, of course I want to punish police who abuse their power. But if I admit that, I know that you left-wing assholes are going to use my admission as an excuse to go ahead and defund the police in general."

So the pattern of statements might need to instead be something like:
  - Left-leaning: "I'm especially concerned with abuses of police power at the moment, but I don't actually want to defund the police, and as long as the right is taking the abuse problem seriously, I'm happy to stand up against the left-wing extremists who are calling to defund the police."
  - Right-leaning: "I'm primarily concerned with making sure that the police can do their job, but I am actually in favor of punishing policement who abuse their power, and as long as the moderate left is pushing back against this defund-the-police nonsense, I'm happy to keep supporting reasonable efforts to punish bad cops."
  - Commonality statement: "Obviously police should be accountable when they abuse their power, but also obviously don't defund the police. I've got my own opinions about which of those is a bigger concern at the moment, but in principle I care about both, and as long as both left and right are bipartisanly working towards both, I'm against the extremists on my own side who are saying nutty lopsided things."


### Same values, different beliefs

i.e. People who would in principle agree with "the other side" if they found out that the facts were different.

These don't exactly resolve the policy conflict, because they still disagree on what the facts are. But at least it transforms the conflict from "you're evil" to "you're misinformed".

#### Examples

  - "If a self-identified transgender person actually has a real medical condition in which the sex of their brain doesn't match the sex of their body, of course I'm in favor of letting them seek whatever medical treatment is available that might help them with that condition. OTOH if they don't actually have that medical condition and have been misled into thinking there's something wrong with their gender when actually their gender is fine and they just have other psychological issues that they need to fix, of course the kinder thing to do in the long run is to treat them the way I'd treat an anorexic person: I love you, but this is a self-destructive life choice."
  - "If it's true that black people really do still face systemic racism in America's justice system, I'm in favor of protesting that and maybe even having riots. OTOH if it's true that there's no systemic discrimination and black people really do just commit more crimes, obviously they should be arrested and so on and it's silly to protest that and downright evil to have riots about it."
  - "If rehabilitating criminals works well, that's great and we should do more of it. OTOH if it doesn't work all that well, don't keep releasing repeat offenders on the foolish hope that they won't do it again."
  - Right: "Schools are actively encouraging kids to become LGBT. That's very wrong." Left: "The Right is delusional, schools aren't pushing the kids in that direction, they're just being understanding and supportive of kids who have completely independently realized that they're LGBT." Common ground: "*If* schools really are pushing kids in an LGBT direction, that's very wrong."

### Misunderstandings of what the other side believes

e.g.
  - A large portion of the right has a strong preference for Western culture, and no particular animus towards anyone for their race. The left mostly seems to just be completely blind both to this culture-vs-race distinction and to the fact that this is what the right believes; they tend to just accuse the right of racism. The point being that there are probably viable common-ground statements like: "I don't care what your skin color is, as long as you're American first. Superficial cultural stuff like celebrating differently-named holidays is fine, but I don't want Sharia law here, I don't want the Indian caste system, I don't want the kinds of corruption and bribery and cynicism-regarding-rule-of-law that we see in Africa and so many other parts of the world. Mass immigration is a problem because it overwhelms the melting pot; we don't want large enclaves of foreigners whose primary allegiance is to some foreign country and who are explicitly trying to make American culture more like those other ones. But small amounts of immigration are fine, and if you're an individual legal immigrant and you've joined the melting pot, great, welcome!" Unlike the abortion example where it's obvious that there's a spectrum and that there might be room for a middle-ground, with this culture-vs-race issue the left isn't even going to recognize that this statement is something that they too believe (IME they tend to focus only on the superficial cultural elements and not really think too much about deeper cultural incompatibilities like the Indian caste system or whatever) or that the right would support it (the left tends to just think "the right is racist"). Like... I don't think the implication-attestation system is going to be able to find this common ground, because without a nudge the left-leaning people aren't going to sign statements like "I don't want Sharia law in America" (even though they probably *don't* want Sharia law in America); they're just going to sign statements like "I hate racism". So how do left-leaning people even discover a statement like this? A right-winger might write the statement, and get support for it from many other right-wingers; after that, maybe it's the right-wingers' job to somehow point left-wingers to it and say "no seriously, this is what we believe, and I suspect you'd agree with it if you thought about it for a minute". (Maybe this is where the [noninflammatory content funding](./noninflammatory-content.md) system comes in. That's exactly what it's for.)

So here, the pattern of statements feels a bit different to me. What matters is not exactly "extreme" vs "moderate", more like "clueless" vs "aha":
  - Clueless left: "I hate racism."
  - Clued-in left: "I hate racism, but I like modern liberal culture, and I'm noticing that a lot of other cultures around the world are incompatible with it. Islamic countries aren't big on freedom for women, India's caste system isn't really compatible with ideals of equality, etc. I still want to treat people of all races equally, but I don't want America to turn into India or Saudi Arabia. I still want to allow immigration, but I don't want to allow so much immigration that they bring their illiberal culture with them."
  - Extreme right: "Indian people suck." (Believed by the left to be a common right-wing position, but IMO it's rare.)
  - Moderate right: "Indian caste system sucks; I've got nothing against Indian people if they assimilate. Etc."
  - Commonality statement: "I like Western culture. Normal Western people on both the right and the left have good reasons to value Western culture as distinct from other cultures like India's or Africa's or Islamic countries', even though we differ on which aspects of Western culture we value more highly. We also differ in how much immigration we'd prefer, but we don't want *too* much, because mass immigration would damage our culture." (Etc. You get the idea.)

The problem here is that *most* of the left falls into the clueless category here; IMO large portions of the left *would* agree with this if they ever let themselves actually seriously consider it, but right now they don't. So Commonality's job here isn't just to highlight an already-existing common-sense majority; it's to help *create* that common-sense majority. (Which maybe just means that this pattern doesn't belong in this file, but I don't know where else to put it. Doesn't matter much.) So what's needed is (maybe with the help of the noninflammatory-content funding system) for the right to have a way to call the left's attention to what they actually believe, and (now that I think of it) maybe even to *create* the "clued-in left" statement. That is: a right-winger writes the above "clued-in left" statement, and also writes a noninflammatory post on social media saying, "Seriously, sane lefties, take a look at this statement; I honestly think you might find that you're willing to sign it. Notice that if you do, you'll also by-implication be supporting this common-ground statement that already has a whole bunch of support-by-implication from this moderate-right statement."


## What these statements are useful for

### Head count

Just counting up how many people have signed the statements. Nice to see that there's actually a lot of support for these common-sense positions, including support from "the other side".

### Funding

- Fundable projects can position themselves aligned with these commonality statements
- If the hidden-majority thesis is right, projects aligned with these positions will attract funding from both "sides" without either side coordinating
- This is the core demonstration of [organic coalition-building](/docs/vision-and-strategy/why-its-better/organic-coalitions.md)

## AI's thoughts on ideas for additional sub-patterns

Agreement on the problem, disagreement on solutions. Both sides agree something is broken but propose different fixes. The commonality statement is the problem statement itself, stripped of any implied solution. E.g.: "Healthcare costs are out of control and ordinary people can't afford to get sick" — left and right both sign that enthusiastically, even though they disagree completely on whether the fix is more government or more market. This is different from #1 because there may be no policy compromise at all — the value is just in making visible that the "other side" genuinely cares about the same problem, which undercuts the "they don't even care about X" narrative.

False bundling. You mention this in the intro (coalition structure bundles issues), but it's not a sub-pattern yet. Sometimes people agree on issue X but are forced to oppose each other because X is bundled with Y in the coalition structure. The commonality emerges by unbundling: stating X on its own, free of party packaging. E.g., "I support both strong environmental protections and gun rights" — a position that's incoherent in coalition-space but perfectly coherent in issue-space, and possibly held by a large chunk of people who never get to express it because they had to pick a team. This is different from the other patterns because the disagreement isn't really about the issue at all — it's an artifact of coalition bundling.

Shared values, different weights. Both sides hold the same two (or more) values — say, freedom and safety, or compassion and accountability — but weight them differently. Unlike #1 (where preferences differ on a single axis), here the commonality is "we both care about both of these things." The commonality statement is something like "Both individual liberty and public safety matter; the question is how to balance them in this specific context." This reframes the debate from "you don't care about X" to "we weight X differently," which is a much less toxic disagreement.
