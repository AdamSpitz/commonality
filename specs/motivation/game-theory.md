# Game Theory reasons for people to use Commonality

## Initial thoughts

In addition to [enthusiastic adoption](./enthusiastic-adoption.md) from people who actually *want* to use a more open system like Commonality, there may be game-theoretic reasons to expect adoption across the political spectrum, not just from whichever side is currently out of power. The argument has several layers that stack on each other:

**Layer 1: Insurance.** Both sides are intermittently out of power. When you're out of power, your funding is vulnerable — your causes get defunded, your charities get audited, your bank accounts get scrutinized. When you're in power, the other side faces the same. So for each side independently, putting funding on censorship-resistant infrastructure is a dominant strategy: you're better off doing it regardless of what the other side does, because you *will* be out of power eventually.

**Layer 2: Better mechanisms.** Independently of censorship resistance, Commonality's mechanisms are just better infrastructure for voluntary funding. Assurance contracts raise more money than "please donate unconditionally" because they solve the free-rider problem. Delegation gets lazy-but-wealthy people to participate who otherwise wouldn't bother. Retroactive funding improves project quality because builders know success will be rewarded. Transparency builds donor trust. Your side raises more money and gets better projects out of it, regardless of who's in power or what the other side does.

**Layer 3: Competitive pressure.** If the other side adopts better funding infrastructure and your side doesn't, they're raising more money, funding more effective projects, and building a track record — all while being immune to suppression when you're in power. You're falling behind on *both* effectiveness and resilience.

**Layer 4: Total public goods increase.** Both sides going onchain isn't zero-sum. The total pool of voluntary public goods funding increases because the infrastructure is less lossy — better mechanisms, less overhead, fewer middlemen. Both sides win in absolute terms.

#### What this looks like concretely

Suppose you're a conservative-leaning community in a province with a progressive government.

**The out-of-power pitch (Layer 1):** "The provincial government just pulled funding from your community's youth mentorship program because your school board won't adopt their preferred curriculum policies. You can spend the next election cycle trying to vote them out — or you can crowdfund the mentorship program on Commonality right now. Set up an assurance contract: if 200 families each pledge $50/month, the program is fully funded. If you don't hit the threshold, everyone gets refunded. Nobody risks anything, and no provincial minister can pull the plug."

**The in-power pitch (Layers 2-3):** Now suppose conservatives win the next provincial election. They don't *need* censorship resistance right now. But the pitch is still compelling:

  - "Your community wants to fund a new trade school. Through government grants, that's 18 months of applications, conditions about what programs you must offer, compliance reporting, and a bureaucrat who can change the terms. Through Commonality, a respected local business owner acts as delegate — people contribute via delegatable notes, she evaluates proposals from contractors, the whole decision chain is transparent. The trade school gets built faster, with less overhead, and you owe nobody any policy compliance for the privilege."
  - "Meanwhile, the progressive communities in the cities are already using Commonality to fund their stuff — mutual aid networks, legal clinics, environmental monitoring. They're building funding capacity that you won't be able to touch when you're out of power again. If you don't build the same capacity now, while things are easy, you'll be scrambling to set it up under hostile conditions when the pendulum swings back."

**The boring-local-stuff pitch (extending to mundane projects):** This pressure extends beyond obviously controversial causes. Even mundane local public goods become politically vulnerable when funded through hierarchical systems — higher-level governments routinely attach policy conditions to funding, turning your town's sidewalk budget into leverage for compliance on unrelated issues. (Example: federal funding withheld from states/cities over immigration enforcement, or provincial funding contingent on institutional DEI policies.) Commonality can't replace that federal money, but it can provide a funding channel with no political strings — because there's no political entity in the chain. So even communities that just want to fund boring local stuff have a reason to build independent funding capacity, as insurance against their mundane projects getting caught up in political disputes they didn't ask to be part of.

#### Adoption dynamics

The realistic adoption path isn't a grand bargain where both sides agree simultaneously. It's a sequential cascade:

  1. The side currently most under threat adopts first, out of necessity.
  2. They discover the mechanisms are also just *better* (Layer 2), so they keep using it even when the political pressure eases.
  3. The other side sees a rival political community raising more money, funding more effective projects, and doing it all through infrastructure they can't shut down.
  4. They adopt it too — maybe framing it differently ("community mutual aid" vs "freedom funding"), but using the same infrastructure.

The side currently in power might try to suppress the infrastructure itself rather than just the causes. But banning voluntary crowdfunding infrastructure is both technically hard (censorship resistance is the whole point) and electorally costly — you're now the side trying to stop people from funding things voluntarily.

#### "But you're paying with after-tax dollars"

The obvious objection: why would anyone voluntarily fund something through Commonality when the government could fund it with tax revenue? You're eating a ~30-40% tax hit compared to getting the government to pay for it.

Several responses:

  - **The realistic alternative usually isn't "government funds it well."** Most of the things people would fund through Commonality are either (a) not being funded at all, (b) being funded with onerous conditions attached, or (c) being funded through private charity (which is also after-tax). The comparison "Commonality vs. perfectly-directed tax revenue" rarely applies in practice.
  - **Government procurement overhead narrows the gap dramatically.** Government-funded projects carry enormous overhead: procurement processes, compliance reporting, administrative layers, political conditions. If a government-funded project costs $1M but $350K is overhead and friction, then a Commonality-funded equivalent at $650K in after-tax dollars is roughly break-even. Government procurement inefficiency is famously bad — estimates of 30-50% overhead for certain categories are common.
  - **Charities can act as Commonality delegates.** A registered charity can accept tax-deductible donations and direct funds through Commonality as a delegate. Donors get their deduction, the charity keeps doing what it's good at (evaluating projects), and the system gets Commonality's mechanism benefits. You lose some censorship resistance (the charity is a legal entity that can be pressured), but for uncontroversial projects that's fine. Communities can maintain both channels — tax-deductible contributions through a charity-delegate for the mundane stuff, direct onchain contributions for things that need to be unstoppable.
  - **Resilience has compounding value.** A project funded through government can be defunded next election cycle. A project funded through Commonality stays funded as long as its supporters keep pledging. Over multiple election cycles, the "tax-efficient but periodically destroyed" approach may actually cost more than "after-tax but stable."
  - **For the stuff that government won't fund regardless, the comparison is irrelevant.** Legal defense for politically targeted individuals, advocacy against the current government's policies, infrastructure for communities the government is hostile to — you're never getting tax-funded support for these. The after-tax cost is the cost of doing it at all.

But honestly: for normal government functions like road maintenance and sewage treatment, Commonality probably isn't going to replace tax funding, and shouldn't need to. The argument isn't "fund everything through Commonality." It's "build independent funding capacity for the things that matter to your community, so that when government funding comes with strings attached — or gets cut entirely — you have a fallback that nobody can take away from you." The after-tax cost is the price of independence, and for many communities dealing with political hostility from higher-level governments, that price is worth paying on the margin — supplement government funding with an independent channel, rather than replacing it entirely.


## More thoughts

Here are some additional game-theoretic pathways that go beyond enthusiasm and early-adopter effects. These are about structural incentives that push rational actors toward adoption even if they're not ideologically excited about it.

1. Tiebout competition between municipalities. Cities compete for residents and businesses. If one city lets residents fund certain services through Commonality and lowers taxes correspondingly (even modestly), it becomes more attractive to mobile residents and firms. Neighboring cities face pressure to match or lose tax base. This isn't enthusiasm — it's the same competitive dynamic that drives tax competition between jurisdictions today. The key insight: the first city to do this gains a competitive advantage, making it a first-mover game rather than a coordination problem. And the move is low-risk for the city because they can start with non-essential services (parks amenities, community events) where failure is painless.

2. Preference intensity revelation. Voting treats all preferences equally — one person, one vote. But public goods funding decisions involve widely varying preference intensities. The person who desperately needs the community clinic and the person who vaguely thinks clinics are nice get the same say. Commonality's mechanisms (pledging actual money, delegating to specialists) reveal preference intensity in a way that democratic voting structurally cannot. This is a mechanism design argument: the system produces more efficient outcomes because it has access to better information. The game-theoretic force is that people with strong preferences are systematically underserved by the voting mechanism and would rationally migrate to a system that weights their intensity. You don't need enthusiasm — you need frustration, which is abundant.

3. Credible commitment and time-horizon mismatch. Governments face a well-known credible commitment problem: they cannot bind future governments to continue funding. A four-year election cycle means long-horizon projects (reforestation, infrastructure maintenance, research programs) face constant defunding risk. Commonality's ongoing pledge model, combined with delegation, creates something closer to a credible commitment — not because any individual pledge is irrevocable, but because the aggregate is more stable than election outcomes. The game theory here is straightforward: if you're a project creator choosing where to seek funding, the platform that offers more predictable long-term support dominates, all else being equal. And project creators choosing Commonality pulls donors toward it too (because that's where the projects are).

4. The principal-agent chain compression. Government spending is the canonical case of Friedman's worst quadrant: someone spending someone else's money on someone else. The principal-agent chain is taxpayer → elected official → department head → procurement officer → contractor. Each link adds slack, information loss, and misaligned incentives. Commonality compresses this: donor → delegate → project. Shorter chains mean less agency loss. The game-theoretic mechanism is that donors who can observe this difference — seeing their Commonality-funded project deliver results while their tax-funded equivalent disappears into bureaucracy — rationally shift marginal dollars toward the more efficient channel. This isn't ideology; it's the same logic that drives people from travel agents to Booking.com.

5. Adverse selection reversal for project quality. Government procurement selects for grant-writing skill and political connections, not project quality. This is a classic lemons problem: high-quality project creators who are bad at navigating bureaucracy exit the government funding market, leaving increasingly mediocre grant-optimized projects. Commonality's retroactive funding mechanism reverses this selection pressure — it rewards demonstrated results, not proposal quality. Over time, the best project creators migrate to the system that rewards what they're actually good at. This pulls donors (because the good projects are there), which pulls more creators, creating a virtuous cycle. The game-theoretic force is differential selection, not enthusiasm.

6. Fiscal crisis as a forcing function. This isn't speculative — governments periodically face austerity, debt crises, and service cuts. Communities that have built Commonality infrastructure before a fiscal crisis weather it dramatically better than those that haven't. The game theory is pure hedging: building Commonality capacity is like buying insurance against government failure. You don't need to believe government will fail — you just need to assign it nonzero probability, which any rational actor must. And critically, building this capacity has positive expected value even if the crisis never comes (because of the mechanism advantages above), so it dominates "wait and see." This is particularly compelling for communities that have already lived through austerity — they don't need to be convinced that government funding is fragile.

7. Strategic complementarity with low switching costs. A crucial structural feature: Commonality and government funding are not mutually exclusive. You can use both simultaneously. This means adoption doesn't require a binary "exit government" decision — people can shift marginal dollars while keeping their existing government services. This turns what would be a coordination game (everyone must switch together) into a series of independent optimization decisions (each person optimizes their marginal dollar). And because each additional Commonality participant improves the system (more delegates, more projects, better price discovery), these individual optimizations are strategic complements — each person's marginal shift makes the next person's marginal shift more attractive. This is the classic network-effect adoption curve, but without the chicken-and-egg problem because the system is useful at every scale.

8. Defection from bundled taxation. Government forces you to buy a bundle: defense + roads + schools + agriculture subsidies + everything else, at a price you can't negotiate, with allocations you can't influence (much). If any individual could unbundle — paying for the services they value and opting out of the rest — most would, because most people disagree with some government spending. Commonality doesn't let you opt out of taxes, but it lets you supplement with unbundled funding for the things you care about most. The game-theoretic dynamic: once people are supplementing, they start noticing that their Commonality-funded services are better (shorter principal-agent chains, better projects), which increases political appetite for actual unbundling — earmarking, tax credits for Commonality contributions, etc. The transition here is demand-pull: demonstrated better outcomes create political pressure for institutional change, rather than requiring institutional change first.

9. Delegate market competition. In government, your representative is a geographic monopolist — you get one, chosen by majority vote in your district. In Commonality, delegates compete for your delegatable notes in an open market. This is the difference between a monopoly and a competitive market for judgment services. The standard economic prediction applies: competitive provision produces better outcomes at lower cost. More concretely, delegates who make good funding decisions attract more delegation; delegates who don't, lose it — on a continuous, revocable basis rather than in four-year cycles. The game-theoretic pressure on delegates is far stronger than on elected representatives, because the feedback loop is tighter and exit is cheaper.

10. Asymmetric information exploitation. In government funding, there's a huge information asymmetry between the people closest to a problem (local community members) and the people making funding decisions (distant bureaucrats/politicians). This asymmetry is why government-funded projects so often miss the mark. Commonality's delegation structure lets information flow more efficiently: local experts become delegates because they have an informational advantage, and that advantage translates directly into better funding decisions, which attracts more delegation. The game-theoretic force is that people with local information are currently unable to monetize it productively (except through lobbying, which is expensive and unreliable). Commonality gives them a direct channel, which is a strict improvement for both the information-holders and the funders.

The common thread in most of these: they don't require anyone to want to replace government. They just require rational actors to respond to incentive gradients — shorter principal-agent chains, better selection for quality, hedging against fiscal risk, competitive pressure between jurisdictions and between delegates. The enthusiasm pathway is "I believe in this vision." The game-theory pathway is "this is just individually rational for me, and it happens to aggregate into a transition."

The ones I think are strongest: Tiebout competition (#1) because it creates pressure between governments rather than requiring citizen initiative; fiscal crisis hedging (#6) because it requires only believing government failure is possible, not likely; and strategic complementarity (#7) because it eliminates the need for a binary switching decision.
