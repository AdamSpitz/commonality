# Better than the "network state" approach, too

Balaji Srinivasan's "network state" idea is actually what originally got me thinking about this. The core insight is genuinely exciting: large numbers of aligned people coordinating online to fund and build things, leveraging their numbers and collective wealth to get real outcomes — potentially even negotiating with mainstream nation-states from a position of strength.

Commonality is an attempt to achieve those same goals, but without requiring a monolithic organization to do it. The network-state vision seems to imagine something like a million-member Discord chat with a shared treasury, shared governance, and some kind of membership boundary. That's essentially just... a government. A new one, maybe a better one, but still a centralized entity with all the coordination costs that come with it — who's in charge, what's the charter, who gets to join, how do we spend the treasury, what happens when we disagree.

Commonality sidesteps all of that:

  - **No need for a giant discussion forum.** X (and every other social platform) already does that job. There's no reason to build another one just to take a census of your movement. Commonality lets you count supporters via statement signatures — you know how many unique humans agree with a given cause, without needing them all in one chat room.
  - **No need for a group treasury.** Commonality allows public-goods-funding to happen even when the funds come from many separate groups and individuals, aggregated per-project through assurance contracts rather than pooled into a single pot controlled by a committee.
  - **No need for shared governance.** Each person delegates to whoever they want, signs whatever statements they believe, and trusts whatever attesters they choose. There's no "network state constitution" to ratify, no leadership election, no membership vote. The system achieves coordination through mechanism design rather than through organizational hierarchy.
  - **No membership boundary.** You don't need to decide who's "in" and who's "out." Anyone can sign a statement, pledge to a project, or become a delegate. The system discovers the commonality among people who happen to agree, without requiring them to formally join anything.

The result achieves what network states promise — large-scale coordination, collective funding power, even [credible threats](../credible-threat.md) that shift negotiating power with governments — but as a *protocol* rather than an *organization*. No one needs to run it, no one can capture it, and it scales without the governance headaches that plague every large group.
