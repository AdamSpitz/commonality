# Other ideas for use cases

This is more about voluntary/enthusiastic adoption, rather than the [game theory](./game-theory.md) stuff.

## Government-related stuff

Some additional transition pathways:

1. Government-as-delegate transition. A local government could itself become a Commonality delegate. Imagine a city council that says "we're allocating $200K/year to parks maintenance — but instead of running procurement ourselves, we'll act as a delegate on Commonality and direct funds toward projects that residents can also contribute to." This lets government gradually shift from sole-funder to co-funder to just-one-delegate-among-many, without any abrupt break. The government gets to reduce its administrative burden, residents get transparency, and the funding channel survives even if the government later pulls out.

2. Matching-funds bridge. Governments already do matching-fund programs ("we'll match private donations 2:1"). A natural bridge: government offers to match Commonality assurance contract pledges for approved categories. This gives people a reason to start using the system (their money goes further), and once they're habituated to the mechanism, the matching ratio can decrease over time without killing the projects — because the assurance contract infrastructure and donor base already exist.

3. Service-contract unbundling. Governments currently bundle together needs-assessment, procurement, delivery, and oversight into one opaque process. Commonality could peel these apart: a local delegate does needs-assessment, project creators handle delivery, onchain transparency handles oversight, and the assurance contract handles procurement. You could start with one function — say, a neighborhood association acts as delegate for road repair projects, while the government still pays for most of it — and gradually expand which functions are handled through the system.

4. Disaster/emergency response pathway. After natural disasters, government response is notoriously slow and bureaucratic. This is an emotionally compelling, low-controversy entry point: "donate to hurricane relief through Commonality, where you can see exactly where money goes and assurance contracts ensure your donation only gets spent if the project is viable." People who first use the system for disaster relief may stick around for everyday public goods.

5. Expatriate and diaspora funding. Diaspora communities often want to fund public goods in their home regions but distrust the local government. Commonality gives them a channel that doesn't route through a government they don't trust. This is a natural use case where government funding is either absent or corrupt, and there's an existing pool of willing funders who currently have no good option. Once the infrastructure exists for diaspora-funded projects, locals start using it too.

6. Regulatory sandbox / pilot programs. Some governments (especially at municipal level) are open to innovation pilots. A city could run a participatory-budgeting experiment on Commonality: "here's $500K of the city budget — residents, use this system to decide how it gets spent." If it works better than traditional participatory budgeting (which is clunky), the scope expands. The government is voluntarily channeling its own money through better infrastructure.

7. Failed-state / underserved-area bootstrapping. In areas where government simply doesn't provide basic services (rural areas, developing regions, neighborhoods with crumbling infrastructure), there's no "transition" needed — the government isn't funding the good in the first place. Commonality fills a vacuum rather than replacing an incumbent. Once it's proven in these underserved areas, it creates pressure on nearby well-served areas: "why are we paying 3x through taxes for worse results than the next town gets through Commonality?"

8. Professional-association pathway. Professional groups (doctors, engineers, teachers) already self-organize around shared standards and sometimes fund shared goods (research, training programs, professional development). They could adopt Commonality as infrastructure for funding profession-specific public goods, bringing in a high-trust, high-income user base that's already comfortable with voluntary collective funding through dues.

9. The "earmark your taxes" wedge. Political movements already push for taxpayer choice in how their taxes are spent. Commonality provides the infrastructure that makes this technically feasible. A political campaign could advocate: "let taxpayers direct 5% of their tax bill through Commonality." Even if that campaign fails legislatively, it introduces the concept and drives adoption of the voluntary system.

10. Insurance/mutual-aid escalation. Start with something that looks like mutual aid or community insurance (pooled funds for unexpected costs — medical emergencies, home repairs after storms). These are clearly individually beneficial and don't feel like "replacing government." But mutual-aid pools are structurally identical to public-goods funding through assurance contracts. Once communities are comfortable with the mechanism for insurance-like use cases, extending it to infrastructure and services is a small conceptual step.

The common thread in most of these: don't pitch it as replacing government, pitch it as supplementing or improving a specific pain point, and let the system's inherent advantages cause gradual migration over time. The ones I find most compelling are the government-as-delegate path (#1), the disaster response entry point (#4), and the failed-state/vacuum path (#7), because they each avoid the "but we already have government for this" objection entirely.
