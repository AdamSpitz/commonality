# Sole-donor to distributed funding: another upgrade path

Like the [tip-jar-to-token upgrade path](./tip-jar-upgrade-path.md) and the [alignment-attestation dial](./alignment-attestation-for-orgs.md), the move from sole-donor funding to distributed funding is a smooth spectrum, not a binary choice. And the argument for "might as well use our rails" applies even at the sole-donor end.

## The spectrum

**Level 1: Sole donor, full control.** A foundation or government agency buys all the tokens in an assurance contract (or there's a 1-of-1 token). This is functionally equivalent to a traditional grant. The funder has full control because they're the only funding source. But they're using standardized infrastructure, so:
  - The funding relationship is transparently recorded onchain.
  - The project has a verifiable track record that's legible to other potential funders.
  - The funder gets leaderboard credit and a standardized accountability trail.
  - No custom grant-management infrastructure to build or maintain.

**Level 2: Primary donor, open to co-funders.** The sole donor buys most of the tokens but leaves some available for others. This is like a matching-fund model: "we'll put up 80%, the community can contribute the rest." The primary funder still has dominant influence (they hold most tokens), but the project starts building a base of community support.

**Level 3: Major donor among several.** The funder is one of several significant contributors. They still have meaningful influence through the size of their contribution, but the project isn't dependent on them alone. The project has real independence.

**Level 4: One of many.** The funder is one contributor among hundreds. No special influence. The assurance contract is working as designed — many small funders collectively reaching a threshold.

Each level is a natural position on the spectrum, and moving from one to the next is frictionless — you just buy fewer tokens next time.

## Why sole donors should use the rails anyway

Even at Level 1, the sole donor gets real benefits from using Commonality rather than a traditional grant:

  - **Standardized infrastructure.** No need to build custom grant-management systems, reporting frameworks, or accountability mechanisms. The blockchain handles all of that.
  - **Verifiable track record.** The funder builds a public, auditable history of what they've funded and how those projects turned out. This is valuable for the funder's own reputation — especially for foundations that want to demonstrate impact.
  - **Project ecosystem visibility.** The project shows up in funding portals, can receive alignment attestations, and is legible to the broader Commonality ecosystem. Even if the funder is currently the only one paying, the project is *discoverable* by others in a way that a private grant isn't.
  - **Upgrade path preserved.** If the funder later decides they're fine with co-funders, or the project creator wants to diversify their funding base, the infrastructure is already there. No migration needed.

And from the project creator's side, using Commonality even with a sole donor means:
  - Their track record is portable. If the sole donor pulls out, the project's history of successful delivery is right there onchain for other potential funders to verify.
  - They're visible to other potential funders from day one. A project that's transparently delivering results on Commonality can attract additional funders organically, even if it started as sole-funded.

## The gentle pressure toward opening up

The system doesn't *force* sole donors to open up. But it creates natural pressure:

  - **Transparency makes concentration visible.** "95% of this project's funding comes from one address" is public information. This isn't a problem per se — but it does mean the funding relationship is legible to the community, which creates soft accountability.
  - **The project creator has an exit option.** A project with a track record on Commonality can attract distributed funding for its next phase, reducing dependence on the sole donor. This is *good* for the sole donor if they actually care about the project's long-term health — but it means the sole donor can't take the project's dependence for granted.
  - **Other funders can co-fund uninvited.** If a project's assurance contract has remaining tokens, anyone can buy them. The sole donor can't prevent others from contributing (short of buying everything themselves). This means the project naturally tends toward distributed funding over time, unless the sole donor actively maintains their monopoly.
  - **The "invisible control" option disappears.** A sole donor who insists on staying *off* Commonality — using private grants instead — is essentially saying "I want my control to be invisible." Which is not a great look, and exactly the kind of thing that transparency is supposed to pressure.

## What this means for institutional funders

The prediction isn't that Commonality will drive away sole donors. It's that it will *welcome* them, give them real benefits at Level 1, and then gently make Levels 2-4 attractive over time:

  - Institutional funders who actually care about outcomes (rather than control for its own sake) will find that co-funding produces better results — more community buy-in, more accountability, more resilience.
  - The best projects — the ones with genuine community support — will tend to attract distributed funding naturally, because their track record is visible and their assurance contracts are open.
  - Institutional funders who adapt (by becoming co-funders or delegates) keep their influence through expertise and reputation rather than through monopoly on funding. This is more durable influence anyway — it survives even when other funding sources appear.
  - Institutional funders who insist on sole-funder control will find that the best project creators prefer the funding source that comes with fewer strings. The funder is left with a negatively-selected pool of projects that *couldn't* attract distributed support.

The same pattern as everywhere else in Commonality: start wherever you are, get real benefits immediately, and the system makes the next step attractive.
