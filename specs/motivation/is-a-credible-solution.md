# This is a credible public-goods-funding solution

TL;DR:
  - Assurance contracts solve the free-rider problem.
  - The delegation system solves the laziness problem.
  - And the rest is simple.

## Why is funding public goods hard?

The core problem of public goods (non-excludable, non-rivalrous) is the free-rider problem. Another basic problem with any sort of collective action is that people are lazy. Commonality has a solution to both of those — and to the more mundane problems beyond them.

## Free-rider problem

Government's solution: tax everyone. Effective but coercive, and the money goes into a general pool where you have very little say over how it's spent.

Commonality's solution: assurance contracts. You pledge conditionally — your money is only spent if enough others pledge too. This solves the same "I would if others would" problem that taxes solve, but without coercion and with per-project granularity. It's not identical (you can opt out, so total funding may be lower), but for causes with genuine community support, assurance contracts can match or exceed what a government program would allocate — especially since government programs are subject to political capture and often don't reflect what the community actually wants.

## Laziness / rational ignorance

Government's solution: representative democracy. Elect someone and hope they spend your tax money well. Works okay, but you get one representative for all issues, for a fixed term, with no granularity and limited accountability.

Commonality's solution: fine-grained delegation. Pick your own delegate(s), per-cause, revocable at any time. You can be just as lazy as you are in a democracy — delegate everything and forget about it — but with better matching (your delegate specializes in the topic you care about) and instant accountability (revoke if they do something you don't like). No coordination problem of "who should we all delegate to" — just delegate to whoever you individually trust.

## Getting stuff done effectively

Government's solution: procurement processes, RFPs, grant review panels. Selects for grant-writing skill and political connections, not project quality. Enormous overhead: compliance reporting, administrative layers, political conditions. Estimates of 30-50% overhead for certain categories are common.

Commonality's solution: retroactive funding. Instead of trying to predict which proposals will succeed, you can reward projects that have *already* demonstrated results. This reverses the selection — it rewards execution, not proposal-writing. Not always applicable (some projects need upfront capital), but where it works it should produce better outcomes. And even for prospective funding, assurance contracts have far less overhead than government procurement.

## Matching supply and demand

Government's solution: RFPs and grant programs signal demand ("here's money available for X"); project proposals signal supply. But both go through a centralized bottleneck — a procurement office, a grants committee — creating delays and gatekeeping.

Commonality's solution: project creation signals supply ("here's work I could do, if there's money for it"); pledged money with declared intents signals demand ("here's money for this purpose, if there's anyone willing to do it"). Both happen permissionlessly. Supply can bring demand into existence or vice versa, without waiting for a committee to process applications.

## Discovery and vetting

Government's solution: grant review panels and planning committees. A small number of people evaluate a large number of proposals. Bottlenecked, slow, subject to political influence.

Commonality's solution: funding portals with a trust network feeding delegates a stream of aligned projects. Discovery is distributed across many individuals (alignment attesters, delegates, the community), each contributing their own knowledge. The system aggregates their judgments per-user rather than requiring everyone to agree on one committee's picks.

## The point

This isn't an ideological libertarian argument for removing public-goods-funding as a function of government. The point is narrower: Commonality is *viable* as an alternative — it addresses each of the core functions that government currently performs for public goods, using different mechanisms that avoid centralized bottlenecks, coercion, and political capture.

In the past we've needed government or big private charity orgs to do this kind of thing. New tech (Internet, blockchains, AI, assurance contracts) makes this new approach genuinely viable.
