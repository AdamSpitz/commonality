(Sometimes worse than nothing, if it causes the Sorta Okay Party to lose to the Awful Party.)
  - Even trying to get your preferred one of the big popular political parties to win the election is a whole bunch of work that has a quite-large chance of amounting to nothing if you lose.
  - Heck, even if your party *does* win the election, voting feels like a waste of time. (We go to a lot of trouble to incept the cultural idea of "voting is a civic duty" and so on, precisely because the election is basically-never decided by one vote and so people notice "I could have stayed home and the outcome would have been the same").
  - Tons of compromise (the political party's platform isn't exactly what you want, your local representative isn't exactly who you want), and pushing to change it has basically no effect.
  - Making a normal (unconditional) donation to some charity project? Great, you put in $100, but they project needed $5000 to be useful and it only got $1700; now you're out $100 and the money hasn't accomplished much.

Whereas every action in Commonality "is what it is": the action takes effect right away and does exactly what it says on the tin:

  - If you're making explicit project-funding pledges, your money goes towards exactly the specific projects you choose. Or if you're delegating your money instead, your money is placed into the hands of the specific delegates you choose (and only until you change your mind), rather than a single district-wide representative whose job is to represent everyone in the district on all topics for four years. If you want to rejigger how your money is allocated, you just do it, and the rejiggering takes effect immediately (as opposed to "I'll pour a ton of time and effort and frustration into trying to persuade the party to change its platform").
    - Of course in this system you can only direct *your own* money, whereas in the legacy system, sure, you pour a ton of time and effort and frustration into trying to persuade the party to change its platform, but if you win then you get to redirect *everyone's* money. To me that just kinda feels like a healthier place to draw the line. If you really want to, you can put effort into trying to influence other people's decisions by arguing about it on Twitter or whatever.

A political party accomplishes *nothing* until it wins an election. Most collective-action systems have a similar "critical mass" threshold — useless until enough people join, which is why most of them never get off the ground.

Commonality is different. Every step along the growth curve produces real, tangible value:

  - **One person** can publish a statement, create a project, or set up an assurance contract.
  - **A handful of people** can fund a small project — a community garden, a local tutoring program, a piece of open-source software.
  - **A few hundred people** can sustain ongoing programs, build delegation networks, and start [discovering organic coalitions](../why-its-better/organic-coalitions.md) with aligned groups they didn't know about.
  - **Thousands of people** can generate [credible threats](../hard-to-stop/credible-threat.md) that shift negotiations with governments and institutions — without spending a dollar.
  - **At scale**, the system becomes a full parallel infrastructure for public goods.

This matters because it means adoption doesn't require faith. You don't have to believe the whole system will take off to get value from participating. The value is immediate — or at least almost immediate, since pledging to an assurance contract is costless if the threshold isn't met, and generates real funding if it is.

## Who wants this *right now*?

Some people don't need to be convinced that Commonality is theoretically better. They need it because their current situation is bad enough that even imperfect new infrastructure is worth trying:

  - **Politically-disfavoured causes** that just got defunded or had bank accounts frozen need censorship-resistant funding infrastructure *now*.
  - **Underserved areas** where government simply doesn't provide basic services — there's no incumbent to replace, just a vacuum to fill.
  - **Diaspora communities** who want to fund public goods in their home regions but distrust the local government.
  - **Disaster response**, where government is notoriously slow and "donate where you can see exactly where money goes, refunded if the project isn't viable" is a compelling entry point.
  - **Anyone staring down 18 months of grant applications** who'd rather just set up an assurance contract.

These people aren't adopting because they believe in the vision. They're adopting because their alternative is worse or nonexistent. (For a fuller treatment of who'd want this, see [enthusiastic adoption](../so-what/enthusiastic-adoption.md).)

## Who wants this because it's just better?

Others aren't in crisis — they'd adopt because the mechanisms are genuinely better:

  - **Charities** can act as delegates, shedding organizational overhead while keeping what they're good at (evaluating projects). See [for established orgs](../ease-of-adoption/for-established-orgs.md).
  - **New charitable efforts** can skip nonprofit incorporation entirely. See [for new orgs](../ease-of-adoption/for-new-orgs.md).
  - **Governments** could act as Commonality delegates for specific programs, reducing administrative burden while giving residents transparency.
  - **Professional associations** already comfortable with voluntary collective funding through dues could adopt Commonality for profession-specific public goods.
