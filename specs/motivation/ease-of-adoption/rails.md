# Commonality as underlying infrastructure rails

Commonality doesn't need to replace existing institutions. It can serve as neutral, open infrastructure that existing organizations — charities, community groups, even government agencies — use as their funding rails.

The analogy: email didn't replace the post office overnight. It provided better infrastructure for a specific function (sending messages), and organizations adopted it because it was cheaper, faster, and more transparent. Commonality does the same for funding public goods: assurance contracts, delegation, retroactive funding, and onchain transparency are just better mechanisms, regardless of who's using them.

What "infrastructure rails" means concretely:
  - **A charity** can use Commonality as its operational backend: accept donations via delegatable notes, direct funds toward projects, and let the blockchain handle the transparency and accounting. The charity keeps doing what it's good at (evaluating projects), but sheds the overhead of running a whole financial organization. See [benefits-established-orgs.md](./benefits-established-orgs.md) for more.
  - **A new charitable effort** can skip the "incorporate a nonprofit, hire an accountant, produce annual reports" step entirely and just start directing funds on day one. Donors can trust the infrastructure even if they don't yet trust the people. See [benefits-new-orgs.md](./benefits-new-orgs.md).
  - **A government agency** can act as a bridge operator or delegate, channeling public funds through better mechanisms while maintaining full auditability. See [bridges.md](./bridges.md) for the tradfi interoperability details.
  - **An individual** with no organizational affiliation can start a project, become a delegate, or contribute — without anyone's permission.

The key property that makes this work as infrastructure: Commonality is permissionless and ownerless. There's no "Commonality Inc." that can change the terms of service, raise fees, or decide your cause isn't welcome. It's more like a protocol than a platform. Organizations that build on top of it don't have platform risk.

This also means Commonality is useful even if it never "takes over." A single church using it for its charitable operations is a win. A single neighborhood using it to fund a playground is a win. The system doesn't need critical mass to be valuable — it just gets more valuable as more people use it.

Even informal funding mechanisms like tip jars can migrate onto Commonality rails with essentially zero friction — see [tip-jar-upgrade-path.md](./tip-jar-upgrade-path.md) for the argument that any form of unconditional donation should convert to token-minting, since the floor case is identical to a tip jar but preserves optionality for both sides.

See also [enthusiastic-adoption.md](../so-what/enthusiastic-adoption.md) for concrete adoption pathways.
