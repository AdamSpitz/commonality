# Won't this also be used for evil?

Ugh, probably.

This system is meant as a neutral tool, usable for helping people coordinate. It's certainly going to end up being used for purposes that we would consider wrong.

I'm not really inclined to try to prevent that (e.g. by moderation/censorship). For one thing, any moderation mechanism would be a chokepoint that a hostile government could capture. A system that can censor the causes you dislike can also censor the causes you care about. That's the whole problem we're solving.

Still, does the use of this system for evil mean that I shouldn't build it?

I still think there are reasons to think the net effect is positive:

  - **Transparency cuts both ways.** Funding evil through Commonality is *more visible* than funding evil through traditional channels. Every contribution, every delegation chain, every alignment attestation is onchain and auditable. If someone is crowdfunding something terrible, that's public information — which is more than you can say about most dark money in traditional politics.
  - **Every general-purpose tool gets used for evil.** Email, encryption, the internet, cash, cars. The question isn't "can this be misused?" (yes, obviously) but "does it create more good than harm?" I think giving ordinary people better tools for funding public goods is net positive by a wide margin.
  - **The current system is also used for evil.** Government spending is routinely captured by special interests, directed toward harmful policies, or used as leverage to coerce compliance on unrelated issues. Charitable organizations have been used for money laundering, tax evasion, and ideological capture. The comparison isn't "Commonality vs. a world without evil" — it's "Commonality vs. the status quo," and the status quo has plenty of its own problems.
  - **Censorship doesn't actually prevent the evil.** People who want to fund bad things will find ways to do it regardless. They already do. What censorship *does* prevent is the good uses by people who are vulnerable to having their funding shut down.

I guess I'm expressing some optimism regarding humanity: I think people are more sane and good than our current systems might suggest, and if people in general had better ability to make public goods happen, the result would be overall more good than not. The bet is that making coordination easier benefits good causes more than bad ones, because good causes typically have broader support and are more constrained by coordination costs.
