# Won't this also be used for evil?

Ugh, probably.

This system is meant as a neutral tool, usable for helping people coordinate. It's certainly going to end up being used for purposes that I (as the creator of the system) would consider wrong.

I'm not really inclined to try to prevent that (e.g. by moderation/censorship). For one thing, any moderation mechanism would be a chokepoint that a hostile government could capture. A system that can censor the causes you dislike can also censor the causes you care about. That's a major part of the problem we're solving.

Still, does the use of this system for evil mean that I shouldn't build it?

I still think there are reasons to think the net effect is positive:

## Transparency makes bad actors *more* visible

Funding evil through Commonality is *more visible* than funding evil through traditional channels. Every contribution, every delegation chain, every alignment attestation is onchain and auditable. If someone is crowdfunding something terrible, that's public information — which is more than you can say about most dark money in traditional politics.

This isn't just a theoretical point. Transparency changes the game in several concrete ways:

  - **Coordination against bad actors is easier, not harder.** In the current system, harmful projects are often funded through opaque channels — offshore accounts, shell charities, dark money groups. Commonality puts everything onchain. A bad actor funding something harmful is now discoverable by journalists, researchers, and the public. The same [organic coalition](./why-its-better/organic-coalitions.md) mechanism that lets people with shared goals find each other also lets *opponents* of a harmful project find each other and coordinate a response.
  - **Delegation chains create accountability.** If a delegate directs funds toward something harmful, everyone who delegated to them can see it, revoke their delegation, and redirect. The damage is limited to those who explicitly trusted that delegate — not, as in government, an entire district that elected someone who quietly slipped provisions into an omnibus bill.
  - **The credible-threat mechanism works in defense too.** Communities can set up [credible threats](./hard-to-stop/credible-threat.md) not just against funding cuts but against harmful projects. "If project X reaches its threshold, we'll fund counter-project Y" is a legitimate use of assurance contracts. The same mechanism that protects good causes from government overreach also lets communities coordinate resistance to bad causes — and transparently, so that everyone can see the stakes.

## Every general-purpose tool gets used for evil

Email, encryption, the internet, cash, cars. The question isn't "can this be misused?" (yes, obviously) but "does it create more good than harm?" I think giving ordinary people better tools for funding public goods is net positive by a wide margin.

## The current system is also used for evil

Government spending is routinely captured by special interests, directed toward harmful policies, or used as leverage to coerce compliance on unrelated issues. Charitable organizations have been used for money laundering, tax evasion, and ideological capture. The comparison isn't "Commonality vs. a world without evil" — it's "Commonality vs. the status quo," and the status quo has plenty of its own problems. At least Commonality's problems are *visible*.

## Censorship doesn't actually prevent the evil

People who want to fund bad things will find ways to do it regardless. They already do. What censorship *does* prevent is the good uses by people who are vulnerable to having their funding shut down. The asymmetry matters: bad actors with resources can route around restrictions, while good actors with fewer resources cannot. Building in censorship capability disproportionately harms the people the system is designed to help.

## The optimistic bet

I guess I'm expressing some optimism regarding humanity: I think people are more sane and good than our current systems might suggest, and if people in general had better ability to make public goods happen, the result would be overall more good than not.

The bet is that making coordination easier benefits good causes more than bad ones, because good causes typically have broader support and are more constrained by coordination costs. Bad actors are usually a small minority with concentrated resources — their problem isn't coordination, it's legitimacy. Good actors are usually a large majority with diffuse resources — their problem *is* coordination. Commonality solves the coordination problem, which disproportionately benefits the majority.

And if it turns out I'm wrong about that — if human preferences, once properly aggregated, produce terrible outcomes — then we have a much deeper problem than the design of a funding protocol. But I don't think that's the world we live in.
