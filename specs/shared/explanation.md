"What the heck is this project?"

  - assurance contracts (AKA Kickstarters) (these are important! our civilization doesn't use these enough! "only pay if the threshold is reached" is super-useful)
  - but you can sell your shares (so it enables VC for public goods) (IOW, you don't need to stress about choosing projects that *will be* successful - your money will genuinely be extremely helpful if you use it to retroactively-fund projects that have *already* shown their worth)
  - and you can just put up some money and delegate the decisions to whoever you want (revocable anytime you want) (and incredibly transparent); it's like having a representative but one you actually chose and can fire instantly
  - and a portal for viewing many such kickstarters, aligned with some cause; a kickstarter appears on your portal if his own personal system (programmed with your preferences) decides that you'd want to see it
  - built using decentralized tech like blockchains so that nobody can squash/coerce/censor it - no bank account to freeze, no company to sue, no CEO to pressure

In one sense you can think of this as a system that can be used by private charities as helpful infrastructure. e.g. Maybe a single church uses it as its tech infrastructure for its charity operations. It's just a straightforward improvement.

But also notice that this system basically enables doing one of the main things that government does: funding "public goods". So:
  - If you've got a project that you were thinking of trying to get government funding for, but you're feeling like "ugh, going through the bureaucracy is awful", maybe you try creating one of these assurance contracts for it instead.
  - If you're feeling despair at the general unfixableness of our government in general, and you've had the thought "what we really need is a new political party that doesn't suck... ugh, that's never gonna happen"...






  - this system is designed to make it as frictionless as possible for each individual to participate, no need to compromise or coordinate or hesitate:
    - trying is costless (your contribution to a project is refunded if the project doesn't reach its funding threshold; you can try delegating to someone and then change your mind anytime, you don't have to wait four years);
    - the system is useful even at small scales (maybe a single church uses it as its tech infrastructure for its charity operations, it doesn't need to be a country-wide political party or whatever in order to be useful);
    - there are no gatekeepers (if you want to start a project you can just start one, you don't need anyone's permission; if you want to attest to a project's alignment with your cause, you can just do that);
    - you don't need to try to find the most-popular group and join up with it; just say what you want to say
