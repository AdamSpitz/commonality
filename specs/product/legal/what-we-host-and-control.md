# What we host, and what we actually control

Two inventories, based on a pass through the subsystem specs, service READMEs, UI config, and contracts (Jul 2026):

1. **The content taxonomy** — every kind of thing that exists in the system that could be Bad, and who authors it. (The risk files say *which laws* things can violate; this says *what the things are*.)
2. **The control audit** — the specific pieces where we exercise operational control, i.e. where the "we're just a protocol" claim currently fails, and the pieces where it genuinely holds.

## Part 1: What kinds of things does the system carry?

### Authored by users (third-party content)

- **Conceptspace statements** — arbitrary displayable documents (markdown) on IPFS. The widest-open content surface in the system: anything writable is expressible (defamation, hate propaganda, instructions, etc.). We pin them and our sites render them. (Both halves of that sentence now have a posture plan for shrinking them — per-vertical/per-author pinning and Tally-as-module; see [statement-hosting.md](statement-hosting.md) and the proposed [self-published-statements design](/specs/tech/subsystems/conceptspace/self-published-statements.md).)
- **Belief signatures (Tally "likes")** — on-chain user actions with essentially no content of their own. The user is the speaker. (But see derived displays below — *aggregating* them is our act.)
- **Project-alignment attestations and success attestations** — permissionless; anyone can submit "project P is aligned with / delivered value for statement S." Genuinely user speech, and the Subjectiv trust graph is the intended filter. Caveat: that's only true for *project* alignment — see content-alignment under "authored by us."
- **LazyGiving project definitions** — IPFS metadata plus contract parameters, authored by creators. The risk shapes: scams, Bad purposes, sanctioned recipients. The *projects themselves* (the real-world work) are off-system, but we host the definition, the funding rails, and the creation flow that makes creating one easy.
- **Content-contracts** (content funding) — a specialized LazyGiving project that points at *external* content (tweets, videos, posts). Two distinctive edges: funding content is a form of promoting it (amplifying Bad content has its own exposure), and **fans can create contracts about a named creator who never consented** — the channel-escrow design holds funds for unclaimed channels, and the creator's veto power only exists *after* they claim. Until then, a third party's name/channel is attached to a funding vehicle they don't know about.
- **Trust declarations** — public on-chain statements "my trust in person U is 37/100." Individually mild, but it's a public social graph with scores attached to people (privacy relevance: [privacy.md](privacy.md)).
- **Mutable refs** — user-owned named pointers to arbitrary IPFS content; whatever they point at is displayable.
- **Content submissions** — user-submitted URL+statement pairs. Note these are not on-chain or on IPFS: they sit **in a JSON file on our server** (`platform-api-service`, `CONTENT_SUBMISSIONS_FILE_PATH`). This is literally user content hosted by us, pre-moderation.

### Authored by us (our speech, today)

- **Implication attestations** — the contract is permissionless in principle, but in practice the only attester is our AI, and its address ships as the default trust in every UI build. "Believing S1 implies believing S2" is editorial — for political statements, sometimes very much so. Until independent attesters exist, these are our speech.
- **Content-alignment attestations** — correction to the intuition that alignment attestations "aren't us at all": the **content-attester and beat-agent publish `AlignmentAttestation` events from our keys** for content items. So the alignment layer is mixed: project-alignment is users; content-alignment is largely our AI.
- **Civility / noninflammatory attestations** — published judgments about named creators' content. Important distinctions (per the [noninflammatory-content spec](/specs/tech/subsystems/content-funding/noninflammatory-content/README.md)): these belong to the **Civility use case**, not to the content-funding infrastructure, which is criteria-neutral ("the reason for funding lives in the attester prompts and alignment attestations, not in the contracts"). Only sufficiently-confident **positive** decisions become on-chain attestations — a creator who fails the criteria gets silence, not a published low score, which substantially softens the disparagement worry. And attestations gate qualification/visibility, not payout: money moves only when humans buy tokens. Still our speech where we run the attester, but "automated opinion, disclosed methodology, positive-only, humans decide funding" is a much better posture than "rating agency."
- **Nudge publications** — curated collections (explorer-curator), graph nudges (implication-graph-nudger), and **synthesized bridge statements (bridge-creator)** — the last being politically flavored content we *generate*, not just curate.
- **Seed content** — a few dozen cause statements we wrote. Permanently attributable to us.
- **Channel-verification proofs** — signed assertions "wallet W owns Twitter channel C," produced by our verifier key. Identity assertions are consequential speech (a wrong one redirects a creator's escrowed money).
- **Derived displays** — supporter counts, "high-profile signers," and contributor leaderboards. These are our aggregations, and "top contributors to projects aligned with cause X" publicly associates named/ENS-resolved people with political causes — sensitive-by-category personal information.

## Part 2: The control audit — where "just a protocol" currently fails

### What genuinely passes

The core belief-and-money layer is honestly protocol-shaped:

- **Beliefs, Implications, AlignmentAttestations, TrustRegistry, AssuranceContract/PrimaryMarket, ERC1155SecondaryMarket, statements** — permissionless, no deployer admin keys found. Anyone can sign, attest, create, trade. (`AssuranceContracts.sol` has an owner, but it's the per-project creator, which is the right party.)
- **Client-side folding** — all business logic runs in the user's browser; there is no server that computes "the platform's view."
- **Subjectiv trust graph** — computed entirely client-side from the user's own declarations; no server-side trust root.
- **On-ramp** — Coinbase is the principal for fiat; we never touch funds.
- **Per-platform content-funding contract sets** — anyone can deploy a competing set for a new platform without permission.

### Where it fails, ranked by severity

1. **We are the identity oracle for content funding (sole-signer role).** `ChannelVerifier` trusts exactly one signer — our `VERIFIER_PRIVATE_KEY` — and creators' escrowed money is released on our signature. This is the single most platform-like piece in the system: a centralized, discretionary, money-gating verification service. The [security-recoverability audit](/workflow/security-recoverability.md) sharpens the stakes: a compromised or malicious verifier owner can install an attacker verifier, forge channel-ownership proofs, and **drain other users' escrowed funds** — the one place in the system where an operator key has (illegitimate but real) power over user money, which a regulator would weigh against the otherwise non-custodial story. Mitigations that already exist: the ownership proofs are public and permanent (tweet/RSS post), so a dishonest verifier is *detectable* after the fact; deploy scripts move contract ownership to a separate admin key (deployer/admin split, cold-key/Safe planned); and verifier checks (`security.onchain-owners`, `security.trust-roots`) watch for owner and trust-root drift. And this is a **deliberate bootstrap with a documented exit**: the [trust trajectory in channel-claiming.md](/specs/tech/subsystems/content-funding/channel-claiming.md#the-trust-trajectory-why-we-are-not-stuck-with-a-central-verifier) (auditable proofs → ENS/DID verification → TLSNotary/zkTLS → per-deployment client-chosen verifiers) shrinks the central verifier to nothing. See "Does the trustless channel-claiming plan change the story?" below for how much that helps and when.

2. **Owner keys on several contracts.** Beyond ChannelRegistry/ChannelVerifier: `CreatorAssuranceContractFactory` third-party limits, `MaterializedContentTokens.addContent`, `CreatorGasTank` parameters — and notably **`DelegatableNotes`: the owner authorizes which primary/secondary market factories delegated funds may be spent through**. We decide where delegated money can go. Each owner key is an "efforts of others" / operational-control fact ([smart-contracts.md](smart-contracts.md) notes that admin keys are what convert published code into an operated service). The [security-recoverability audit](/workflow/security-recoverability.md) has already triaged these: `ContentRegistry`'s owner is the *factory contract*, not a human (protocol-internal access control, not a trust lever); `setRecurringPledgeRegistry` and `setVetoWindowDuration` are slated for elimination; ownership of the rest moves to a separate cold admin key at deploy. The genuinely-governed remaining surface is the factory-authorization sets and `setVerifier`/`setTrustedVerifier` (irreducible — it exists for key rotation — with the trustless trajectory as its long-term exit). The governance model (multisig vs. timelock, M-of-N attesters) is still undecided; from the legal angle, note that documented key governance is *operator-conduct* evidence cutting both ways: it shows control (platform), but it's also exactly the "reasonable procedures" a platform is supposed to have.

3. **Trust-root defaults baked into every UI build.** Users *can* reconfigure, but the shipped defaults are the product: `VITE_DEFAULT_NUDGERS` and `VITE_CSM_MEDIATOR_NUDGER` (which voices users hear), default attester addresses from env (whose implication graph they see), the known-platform-contract-set list (which factories get a trust indicator vs. a warning — an explicit legitimacy designation the content-funding spec deliberately "pushes upward into UI configuration"), `VITE_NONINFLAMMATORY_TOPIC_CID` (Civility's defining criteria is a statement we chose), every `VITE_*_CONTRACT_ADDRESS` (which deployments count as "the" system), and the default indexer/platform-api/IPFS-gateway endpoints. Configurable-but-defaulted is the classic gap between affordance and fact ([operator-posture.md](operator-posture.md)).

4. **We author the entire judgment layer in practice.** Only one implication attester, one content attester, our beat agents, our nudgers/curators exist. The Subjectiv design is a real defense for project-alignment attestations — but note the first-run behavior: an empty trust graph **shows all attestations unfiltered** until the graph fills in, and content-alignment/civility judgments don't go through Subjectiv at all. Until independent attesters/nudgers exist, the judgment layer is us ([multiple-providers.md](multiple-providers.md) ranks fixing this first).

5. **Server-side chokepoints we operate.** The content-submission queue (a file on our server, operator-clearable, rate-limited) is a pre-moderation gate on what enters content evaluation. The platform-api rate limits (resolve/verify/submission/onramp) are allocation decisions about who can use the system and how fast. The on-ramp session endpoint means we gate entry to fiat contribution even though Coinbase moves the money. The explorer-curator has an operator-forced-review lever.

6. **The indexer defines the visible universe.** Ponder watches an env-configured, fixed list of contract addresses — our deployments. Every UI using our event cache sees exactly (and only) the world we index; a competing deployment of the same contracts is invisible by default. Low editorial content, but it's the quiet answer to "which instances are real."

7. **Naming and hosting.** `*.commonality.works` DNS, the two Cloudflare gateways, IPFS pinning of statements and UI builds, and gas-sponsorship parameters (the sponsored-gas contracts exist; whoever funds and parameterizes gas tanks is allocating subsidy — note the intended posture in [sponsored-gas.md](/specs/tech/sponsored-gas.md) Decision 3 that Commonality/Adam funds *no* tanks, leaving only the owner-held cap/floor parameters, not per-project funding, on our side).

### Does the trustless channel-claiming plan change the story?

The [trust trajectory](/specs/tech/subsystems/content-funding/channel-claiming.md#the-trust-trajectory-why-we-are-not-stuck-with-a-central-verifier) is real architecture (pluggable `IChannelVerifier`, per-platform deployments), not hand-waving. Assessment of how much it changes the legal picture:

- **What it changes eventually: a lot.** At the end state — ENS/DID and zkTLS verifiers, per-deployment client-chosen trust — the identity oracle stops existing, and content funding's worst platform feature (a discretionary, money-gating verification service) becomes protocol-shaped. Finding #1 above would be downgraded from "structurally a platform" to "history."
- **What it changes today: something, but less.** The usual affordance-vs-fact rule applies ([operator-posture.md](operator-posture.md)): the trajectory is explicitly unscheduled and demand-gated, so at the moment of scrutiny we are still the sole verifier. What *does* count today is step 1 of the trajectory: proofs are public and permanent, so verification is auditable rather than blind trust — that converts "trust us" into "misbehavior is detectable," which is good-procedures evidence even while centralized. (The gap the spec itself notes: on-chain `verifyChannel` checks only our signature, not the underlying public proof, so detection is after-the-fact.)
- **What it doesn't change at all.** The escrow-for-unclaimed-channels pattern is orthogonal to verifier trust and survives every step of the trajectory: the system still holds money earmarked for a named person who hasn't consented to or heard of the system — a consent/publicity issue, not a trust-root issue (mitigated by the creator veto and third-party creation fee, which are good documented procedures worth keeping prominent).

An important layering distinction (which an earlier draft of this doc got wrong): the **content-funding infrastructure carries no judgment layer of its own** — contracts, registry, escrow, and claiming are criteria-neutral. The AI judging (civility attestations) belongs to **Civility**, the intentionally opinionated movement built *on top of* that infrastructure — the general content-attester deployed with persona-specific prompts and a topic CID, with multiple personas (neutral, left-evaluates-right, right-evaluates-left) anticipated from day one and user-selectable in Settings. This means the two layers deserve different postures: once the verifier goes trustless, the content-funding *infrastructure* genuinely approaches protocol status; **Civility** is an editorial product and should be owned as one — the same "openly opinionated operator" posture as the community-run-UI model in [operator-posture.md](operator-posture.md). Some centralization in Civility is acceptable *because* it claims to be a movement with values, not neutral infrastructure. (Small leak to watch: the noninflammatory topic CID ships in shared UI runtime config and is used by a shared conceptspace component, so the Civility criteria bleed slightly into non-Civility domains.)

So: the honest framing mirrors the community-UI story — right end state, and its *existence* justifies the `setVerifier` lever as transitional rather than permanent, but the launch-era posture for content funding should still be deliberate platform posture. The trajectory strengthens the story most if at least step 2 (ENS/DID verifier for one platform) actually ships before mainnet — one deployed trustless verifier converts the whole trajectory from affordance to demonstrated fact.

### The honest summary

The protocol/platform line runs *between subsystems*, not around the whole system. Conceptspace + LazyGiving + Delegation + Subjectiv (minus the DelegatableNotes factory-authorization keys and the baked-in defaults) are genuinely close to protocol. **Content funding is structurally a platform today**: centralized identity verification gating escrowed money, a server-side submission queue, per-platform legitimacy lists in UI config. That's not an accident — verifying "this wallet owns this Twitter channel" intrinsically requires an off-chain trusted party (until the trustless trajectory ships) — but it means the protocol claim should not be made about content funding in its current form, and the compliance posture for that subsystem should be platform-posture from day one. **Civility is a different kind of thing**: an intentionally opinionated editorial product layered on criteria-neutral infrastructure — its right posture is not "protocol" but "openly editorial operator," which is a defensible position on its own terms (see the layering distinction above).

Remediation directions (details in [multiple-providers.md](multiple-providers.md)): ship at least one trustless verifier from the [channel-claiming trust trajectory](/specs/tech/subsystems/content-funding/channel-claiming.md#the-trust-trajectory-why-we-are-not-stuck-with-a-central-verifier) before mainnet; finish the owner-key triage already underway in [security-recoverability.md](/workflow/security-recoverability.md) (eliminate the two cheap levers, decide the multisig/timelock model); publish alternative-provider documentation so the defaults are visibly defaults; move the submission queue to the UI-operator layer; fix the Subjectiv first-run to not show unfiltered attestations.
